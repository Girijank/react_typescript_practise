点心只为你 Diǎnxīn zhǐ wèi nǐ Treats Just For You

There are many types of desserts in the Chinese diet, with a vast diversity by regions or ethnic groups. Let’s have a quick tour of some of the most popular or famous desserts in the Chinese diet.

©iStock

Mooncake

月饼 Yuèbǐng

A mooncake is a traditional Chinese dessert consumed during family celebrations on the Moon Festival (also called Mid-Autumn Festival since it’s in mid-autumn each year). It has a crispy pastry covering with sweet and solid fillings made of items such as red beans, lotus, pumpkin seeds, egg yolks, and even dried meat.

©Shutterstock

Tangyuan

汤圆 Tāngyuán

Tangyuan (glutinous rice balls) are usually consumed as part of the family celebration on the Chinese Lantern Festival (the 15th day of Chinese New Year celebration). It’s made of glutinous rice flour, with or without fillings. The fillings, if any, can be sweet pastes such as red bean paste, sesame paste, or even chocolate paste. Tangyuan symbolizes family unity because of its enclosed and round shape.


©Shutterstock

Tang Hulu

糖葫芦 Táng húlú

Tang hulu, also known as Bingtang hulu, is a sweet snack popular in Northern China. It’s usually mountain hawthorns (or other fruits such as strawberries or grapes) covered in hard sugar syrup and placed on skewers. It came into being about 800 years ago and is still popular around Northern China nowadays.

©Shutterstock

Egg Tart

蛋挞 Dàntǎ

Egg tart is a type of custard tart that is popular in Southern China, especially Cantonese-speaking regions such as the Guangdong province and Hong Kong. It has a pastry outer cover with egg custard in the center. It is a common dish for dim sum, which is a breakfast/lunch refreshment popular among people in Hong Kong region.


©iStock

Grass Jelly

仙草 Xiāncǎo

Grass jelly is a jelly-like dessert popular among East Asia regions including Taiwan, especially during the summer when the grass jelly is served chilled. It is made by boiling stalks and leaves of Chinese mesona, a type of mint family plant, and cooled with starch to be jelly-like. It has a mildly bitter taste and is usually served with toppings such as fruit. When it is served in warmer temperature, it tastes like a sweet soup and is called “burnt grass jelly.”

©Shutterstock

Dragon Beard Candy

龙须酥 Lóngxū sū

Dragon beard candy is a traditional Chinese dessert made of peanuts, coconut, sesame seeds, sugar, and glutinous rice flour. It’s known as the Chinese version of cotton candy. It’s made by stretching the rice flour dough into small and thin strands. Such strands, when eaten, can stick to one’s face easily, and that’s how it gets its name.


Besides the items introduced above, there are many more desserts in the Chinese diet.
