How to use 多少钱 duōshǎo qián (how much) in a sentence

In 多少钱 duōshǎo qián (how much), the character 钱qián means “money.” When asking the price, use the structure “item + 多少钱duōshǎo qián + number + measure word” or “number + measure word + item + 多少钱 duōshǎo qián.”

For example, “How much for an apple?” can be:

苹果多少钱一个？Píngguǒ duōshǎoqián yīgè?

or

一个苹果多少钱？Yīgè píngguǒ duōshǎoqián?

Note: You are going to use numbers for prices and paying the bill. You can review saying one-digit numbers in Chinese. You can also review saying two-digit numbers in Chinese.

Currencies in Chinese-speaking Regions

When you travel, it is important to know local currencies and their exchange rates with USD. Below are the abbreviations of currencies in some Chinese-speaking regions.

RMB – Renminbi (currency in P.R. China)

HKD – Hong Kong dollar (currency in Hong Kong)

TWD – Taiwan dollar (currency in Taiwan)

SGD – Singapore dollar (currency in Singapore)

MYR -- Malaysian Ringgit (currency in Malaysia)

Tip or No Tip

Tipping is generally considered uncommon in Chinese-speaking areas. In some traditional restaurants, it may be considered disrespectful.

At high-end modern restaurants or hotels where Westerners commonly frequent, it is acceptable to tip about 10–20% of your bill.

Paying Digitally

Common methods of payments in Chinese-speaking regions include credit card and cash. However, it becomes more common to pay digitally, using smartphone apps by scanning QR codes. Companies Alipay and WeChat are introducing digital wallets and digital payments.
