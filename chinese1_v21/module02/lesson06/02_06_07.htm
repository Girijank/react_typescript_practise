借来的食物 Jièlái de shíwù Borrowed Food

Do you know that many food items in China were imported or learned from other cultures? The names of such food items also became borrowed words in the Chinese language. For example, the word 奶酪 nǎilào (cheese) is also called 起司 qǐsī, which is the direct translation of “cheese” by sound. It’s similar to how we use words from other cultures or languages in English, such as “wonton” (Chinese), “sushi” (Japanese), “lemon” (Arabic), and “cookie” (Dutch).

There are food items in Chinese culture that are similar to their western counterparts. Let’s take a quick look at such items.

Chinese Pancake/Pizza

Also known as 葱油饼 cōngyóubǐng (Chinese scallion pancake), it is pan-fried so it’s crispy with an appealing aroma. It is made from flour and green onions. The Chinese pancake is one of the most common street foods in China and is often considered the ideal breakfast for people all around China.

Chinese Hamburger

Also known as 肉夹馍 ròujiāmó (meat in a bun), it is a type of street food from the cuisine of Shanxi province in northwest China. Pork is most commonly used as the stuffing, which is stewed with marinating sauce for hours. It is said that over 20 types of seasonings are usually added to the marinating sauce.

Chinese Spaghetti

Also known as 面条 miàntiáo (noodles), it is an integral part of the Chinese diet and is a staple. The makings of noodles vary in different regions—many versions of which are soup-based. Noodles are mostly made of flour as a type of pasta, while in some regions, rice noodles are also very popular.
