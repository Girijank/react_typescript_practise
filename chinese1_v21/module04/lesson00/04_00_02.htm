International Chinese — A Quick Guide to Taiwan

By now you’ve learned how Chinese is spoken and used in several regions such as Northern China, Hong Kong, and Macau. Now let’s explore how the Chinese language is applied in people’s everyday life in Taiwan. We will also learn about the region’s culture and its appreciation of the Chinese language.

Introduction of Taiwan

Taiwan (台湾 Táiwān) is an island located in the western Pacific Ocean in East Asia. It has an oval shape like a potato, with an area of 35,808 square kilometers (about the same size as the state of Maryland). It has a population of 23 million people and is considered one of the most densely populated regions in the world.

Climate and capital

The climate in Taiwan is marine tropical. It has hot and humid summers, lasting from June to September, and the winters in Taiwan are mostly mildly cold. There is typhoon season in Taiwan from July to September. Taiwan also has tall mountains, which make beautiful scenery for sightseeing.

Taiwan’s capital, Taipei, is a vibrant modern city, with a rich culture and abundant social activities and entertainment.

Taiwanese cuisine

Taiwanese cuisine has been influencedby various regions in Asia. Taiwan is also well-known for its various types of delicious street snacks or foods. Some of the most desired foods are beef noodle soup, braised pork rice, oyster vermicelli noodles, and Gua Bao.

Taiwanese Mandarin

Mandarin Chinese is widely spoken in Taiwan. It is called Taiwanese Mandarin, which is slightly different from the Mandarin Chinese spoken in mainland China. There are some slight differences in vocabulary, pronunciation, and grammar between the two. Overall, they are mutually understandable.

Traditional Chinese characters and writings

In Taiwan, people use traditional Chinese characters for writing or printing, which is more complex than the simplified Chinese characters that we are learning in this course. For pronunciation annotations, people in Taiwan uses a system called Bopomofo as compared to pinyin.

In Taiwan, many reading materials, especially books, are printed in vertical lines, and read from top to bottom, then right to left. It is the traditional way of reading or writing Chinese in the ancient Chinese culture, which is now well-preserved in the region.
