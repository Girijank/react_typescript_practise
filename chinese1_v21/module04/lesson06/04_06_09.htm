文化一瞥 Wénhuàyīpiē Culture Blip

Minyi Wu is a high school student from Taiwan. During this Spring Festival, Minyi’s cousin, Karen Lu from New York, came to visit. They were happy to see each other, and they had a very engaging conversation, comparing schools in their regions. Let’s take a peek at what they discussed.

Note : You don’t need to understand the whole narration in Chinese, but you should try to catch familiar words while listening and get familiar with the ups and downs of Chinese speech.

凯伦，你好！很高兴见到你！我们学校每天上午八点上课，下午五点结束。我星期一有科学课，我需要笔记本。星期三有艺术课。我会画小猫。星期五有英文课，我会读英文和说英文。我们在学校穿制服。我们不可以穿凉鞋。

Hello, Karen! It’s very nice to see you! My school starts at 8 a.m. every day and ends at 5 p.m. I have science class on Mondays, and I need notebooks. There is art class on Wednesdays. I know how to draw kitty cats. There is English class on Fridays, and I can read and speak English. We wear uniforms at school. We are not allowed to wear sandals.

敏仪，你好！我们每天上午七点三十分到学校，下午三点结束。我星期二上中文课。我会听中文和说中文。星期三有我最喜欢的音乐课，我练习唱歌。 星期四上体育课，我要穿球鞋。我们不需要穿制服。天气暖和的时候，我们在学校可以穿裙子。

Hello Minyi! We arrive school at 7:30 a.m. every day, and school ends at 3 p.m. I attend Chinese class on Tuesdays. I know how to listen to and speak Chinese. On Wednesday, there’s my favorite music class, and I practice singing. I attend PE class on Thursdays, and I need to wear sneakers. We don’t need to wear uniforms. When it’s warm, we can wear skirts at school.


Take a look at a little more about Minyi’s school life in Taiwan as compared to Karen’s in New York.? Read on, this time in English.

Urban Schools in Taiwan

Minyi and her family live in Taipei in Northern Taiwan. She goes to a high school about a five minute walk from her home. Every day she brings lunch box to school. Some of her classmates buy lunch from school or order food from restaurants nearby. Uniforms are required, and her school has strict dress codes. She is graduating from high school soon, so she has a full class schedule and not much time for sports or outdoors activities. Her goal is to go to the National Taiwan University.

Urban Schools in the U.S.

Karen and her family live in Flushing, New York. She goes to a junior high school near her home. Her mom drops her off at school and picks her up every day. She has lunch at school. No uniforms are required, but they have dress codes. For example, no sandals are allowed. Karen is on the school track team and they practice every week.

Rural Schools in Taiwan

Minyi’s friend Jinyu lives in Nantou County in Central Taiwan. It’s a rural region where boarding schools are common. Young children, even only 4–5 years old, go to boarding school. Uniforms are generally required. Students have lunch at school, and they can purchase snacks or essential items at the school stores. When there are no classes, students can study in their dorms or classrooms.

Suburban Schools in the U.S.

Karen’s friend Katie lives in the New Jersey suburbs where she goes to a nearby public school. It’s a day school. Katie and her classmates go to school by walking, biking, riding a school bus, or having a parent drop them off. Uniforms are not required, and they have lunch at school as well. School stores are also available. When there are no classes, students can study at the school library or at home.

Think and Apply

What about telling your friend or family members your school’s dress code? It will be cool! Now review the content in this lesson again, and then share the following with your friend or family member:

  • Say two or three clothing items your friend or family member is wearing.
  • Describe what you are wearing in a complete sentence, using 穿 chuān (to wear).
  • Describe what you can and cannot wear to your school, using 可以 kěyǐ (may/can).
  • Explain the basic practice in clothing in Chinese schools and compare with your own school.
  • Explain how to pronounce initial “m” and final “uan” in Chinese and how to compare it to similar pronunciation(s) in your own language.
