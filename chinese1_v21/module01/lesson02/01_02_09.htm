Yours or Mine?

In the previous page, we learned the possessive adjective particle “的 de.” It is used after any noun or pronoun to indicate possession. For example, for all the pronouns we have learned so far, a “的 de” can be added after them. Here are some examples.

  • 我的 wǒde (my/mine)
  • 你的 nǐde (your/yours)
  • 他的 tāde (his)

The magical word “的 de” can also be added to a name or object to indicate possession. For example, “Keysie’s” would be“Keysie 的 de,”meaning something belonging to Keysie. (For now, we don’t have to worry about translating English names into Chinese.) “ 学校的 xuéxiào de” means (something) belongs to the school. In the next lesson, we’ll learn more about how “的 de” can be used to indicate attributes.

Let’s have a quick practice on “的 de.”

Everything on the Phone

Have you heard of a Chinese mini-movie called《疯狂的手机 Fēngkuáng de shǒujī》(The Crazy Mobile Phone)? It tells the story of how a mobile phone affected the lives and relationships of three people. It’s an example of what an important role phones play in Chinese people’s everyday lives.
