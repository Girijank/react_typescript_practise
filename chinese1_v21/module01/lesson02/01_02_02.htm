发音威力 Fāyīn wēilì Pronunciation Power

You have already had an overview of all the initials, finals, and tones in the Chinese pinyin notation system. Starting with this lesson, we’ll tackle these elements one by one. By the end of the course, we’ll cover all the initials and finals, as well as their combinations with the different tones. Remember to keep practicing your pronunciation as often as you can.

声母韵母 Shēngmǔ yùnmǔ Initials and Finals

In this lesson, we will learn initial “h” and finals “o” and “ua.”

An important note: Chinese initials and finals have fixed sounds that don’t change, no matter where they are placed. This is similar to Spanish pronunciations, but not so much to English.

Initial “h”

This initial sounds like “h” in English, as in “how” or “head.” You’ve seen “h” in action several times by now in words such as “你好 nǐhǎo (hello)” and “男孩 nánhái (boy).”

Final “o”

This final sounds like “all” in English, as in “hall” or “mall.” It’s used in words such as “我 wǒ(I/me).”

Final “ua”

This final sounds like “woo-ah” in English. It’s used in words such as 电话 diànhuà (telephone).

注意音调 Zhùyì yīndiào Watch Your Tones

Tones are essential in Chinese pronunciation. The same initial and final combination, with different tones, can mean totally different things. For example, pinyin “ni” with the 3rd tone “你 nǐ” means “you,” while the 2nd tone “泥 ní” means “mud.” Got to get our tones right!
