正式的你 Zhèngshì de nǐ Formally You

It’s very important in the Chinese culture to know how to properly address people in formal occasions. People are usually addressed by their titles in non-professional social gatherings, such as weddings or banquets and by their professions in professional meetings, such as doctor’s appointments or academic conferences.

In Chinese, people’s names (can be either last name or full name) are placed before the title or profession, not after.

Here Comes the Bride:

  • In mainland China, women usually keep their maiden name after marriage and don’t take their husband’s last name.
  • They should be addressed by their maiden name when using professions and titles. For example, when a female teacher in China 王老师 Wáng Lǎoshī (Teacher Wang) gets married, she is still called 王老师 Wáng Lǎoshī (Teacher Wang).
  • In some regions like Hong Kong or Macau, married women add their husband’s last name before their maiden name, and in that case, they should be addressed by their husband’s last name. For example, if a woman from Hong Kong whose maiden name was 刘 liú gets married to a guy whose last name is 李 Lǐ, then the woman’s last name is changed to 刘李 Liú Lǐ,
