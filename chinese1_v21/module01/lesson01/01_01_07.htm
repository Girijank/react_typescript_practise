你的名字我的姓氏 Nǐde míngzi wǒde xìngshì
Name Please

Have you heard of a song called 你的名字我的姓氏 (Nǐde míngzi wǒde xìngshì)? It’s a classic Chinese song by Hong Kong singer 张学友 Zhāng Xuéyǒu (Jacky Cheung). The title of the song means “Your first name, my last name”—which happens to be what your next set of vocabulary is all about. Let’s move one step closer to Chinese pop culture and having a conversation.

What’s in a Chinese name?

Now that you can ask for people’s names, please note that when someone asks you “你叫什么名字? Nǐ jiào shénme míngzi?” (What’s your name?), you can respond with either your first name (informal) or your full name (formal), depending on the occasion. However, when someone asks you “你姓什么? Nǐ xìng shénme?” (What’s your last name?), you respond with just your last name.

A typical Chinese name goes as “last name + first name.” For example, “Jack Smith” in the Chinese culture would be known as “Smith Jack.” Here are some examples of Chinese :

Pinyin Chinese name Last name First name
Zhào Míngjié 赵明杰 赵 明杰
Lǐ Chén 李晨 李 晨

Note:

  • In the Chinese culture, people don’t have middle names.
  • A Chinese last name usually has one character; some very rare last names have two characters.
  • In modern Chinese society, some people have both their parents’ last names combined, therefore also two characters.
  • A Chinese first name can have either one or two characters.
