享受音乐 Xiǎngshòu yīnyuè Enjoy the Music

You’ve just learned some vocabulary on leisure activities. How awesome! Now let’s get in more details on one of the categories—music—to see what types of music are enjoyed in Chinese-speaking regions.

Chinese Traditional Music – Musical Instruments

Chinese traditional music is played with four types of musical instruments:

  • Woodwind—such as dizi (Chinese flute) and suona (double-reeded horn)
  • Percussion—such as Chinese gong
  • Bowed strings—such as erhu and banhu
  • Plucked and struck strings—such as pipa and ruan

Traditional Chinese Music

Chinese traditional music generally promotes simplicity and tranquility. It includes the following genres:

  • Han music: Han music is mostly based on the Chinese language. The playing of it involves musical instruments playing a single line of melody, accompanied by lyrics. It focuses on the combination of the tones of the melody, the meaning of the lyrics, and the feeling of the players and listeners.
  • Chinese opera: There are a total of 368 forms of Chinese opera that currently exist. They are differentiated by regions, historical periods, and ethnic groups. Among them, the most popular and dominant form is Peking Opera, also known as Beijing Opera, that combines music, vocal performance, acrobatics, and dance.

Traditional Chinese Music - Continued

Chinese folk music: Chinese folk music has many forms that vary across regions and ethnic groups. For example, pingtan is an artistic and musical performance with a local dialect. It originates in Suzhou in Southern China. It’s performed by a man and woman, each of whom is accompanied by one instrument such as the pipa. The performance usually tells an elaborate story with props such as fans or handkerchiefs.

Modern Chinese Music

Modern Chinese music also has many genres. Below are a few major ones:

  • Pop music: Chinese pop songs are mostly mellow and sentimental. People find resonance in pop songs during their happy or sad times. There are influential contemporary singers throughout Chinese-speaking regions such as Jay Chow, Jacky Cheung, and Fay Wong.
  • Hip hop and rap: Hip hop and rap are recently becoming more and more popular in Chinese-speaking regions. There are rising stars in this genre such as Higher Brothers and VAVA, both hailing from Chengdu, China.
  • Rock and heavy metal: Rock and heavy metal had their heyday when first introduced in China. They quickly grasped the hearts of the young generation. Cui Jian, an '80s rock star, is considered the forefather of Chinese rock.
