Africans in Rome

Italy was a country of immigrants. Before the Roman rule, North Africans had immigrated into Italy and helped build the Etruscan empire. The Romans called the Africans, Muurs. Other Muurs, or Black people, were aboriginal to Europe or descended into Italy from all over Africa, including Egypt, Moracco, Nigeria, Nubia, and Libya to name a few places. They maintained shrines and priesthoods throughout the Empire and had their own shadow military army. Depicted on Trajan’s Column, you’ll see images of the Muurish calvary with their braided hair fighting with the Romans in Dacia.

Who were the Muurs? They were people from the north African province of Mauretania (modern-day Morocco and Algeria). Classical literature notes that they revolted against the Roman Empire in 24 CE. Over the centuries, the Moors had invaded European countries and Spain, which came under Moorish control in the beginning of the 700s CE. Their name is derived from the Latin term maurs. In medieval times, the name transformed into moro (French), maure (Portuguese), maur (Romanian), moors (English). The name then became more closely associated with Muslim people, and during the Crusades it had a derogatory meaning of “infidel.”
