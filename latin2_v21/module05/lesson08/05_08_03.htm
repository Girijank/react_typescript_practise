Real Life Gladiator

Famous movies about gladiators and Roman politics feature the Roman emperors. Two of these emperors, Marcus Aurelius and Commodus, have become huge stars on the big screen! Let’s give them another close—up shot…

Marcus Aurelius. Reign: 161 CE–180 CE

I, Marcus Aurelius, am the harmonious emperor of Rome! I was a very calm individual who wrote a book called the Meditationes which outlines my ideas on how to live a peaceful, harmonious, more stoic lifestyle. As a follower of stoic philosophy, I made sure to treat my citizens with care; I did not increase taxes and I peacefully squashed a coup from Avidius Cassius against me. I resettled 11,000 Danubians in Germany and made them responsible for protecting Rome in this region. Suo mente, the only bad thing I did was name my son, Commodus, as my heir.

Commodus Reign: 176 CE–192 BCE

I, Commodus, sole son of Marcus Aurelius, am now the Roman emperor! Yes, call me Caesar! I am 19 years old and ready to run the world! First, I will sign a peace treaty with the Marcomanni and Quadi, which my people won’t agree with. Then, my sister will be accused of attempted assassination of a Praetorian Guard. Post id, de me solo putam —about how great I am, how I remind myself of the hero Hercules, how I think I am a great gladiator and beast hunter. In fact, I’ll constantly go to the gladiatorial games where I’ll defeat approximately 1000 gladiators (according to me only). Dare you question me? I loved the games so much that I only wanted to wear my gladiator uniform around town. Also, let’s rename Rome, “Colonia Commodiana” because, naturally, I run this city! Did I go too far with that? Well, the people did grow tired of me and my so–called “instability,” assassinated me, and wiped me from their memory! Such a cruel fate!

Let’s learn what these two built in the Roman Empire!

Arch of Marcus Aurelius, Libya

Triumphal arch in Tripoli, Libya, was erected in 165 CE. Diana and Apollo appear on the two front pediments in a two–horse chariot pulled by griffons and sphinxes.

Column of Marcus Aurelius, Rome

Located in Rome and built between 176 CE–193 CE, the column celebrated his triumph over the Marcomanni, Quadi, and Sarmatians in the year 176. Modeled after Trajan’s Column, it contains a spiral relief, depicting his enemies conquered in war. The column still stands today in the Piazza Colona in Rome!

Villa of the Quintilii Nymphaeum, Rome

A large sprawling villa which extended between the Via Appia and the Via Antica, it was built in 151 CE by the Quintilii brothers. Commodus then expanded it and made it his home. It contained thermal baths, large windows, and illustrious marble.

Arch of Commodus, Algeria

Built in the Roman province of Algeria between 180 CE–192 CE, there were once two triumphal arches, one to Commodus, and the other to Septimius Severus.
