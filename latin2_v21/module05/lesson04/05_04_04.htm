I am a Flavian Emperor!

Reign: 79 CE–81 CE. I am known for:

  • the Coliseum
  • 100 days of games
  • the Mt. Vesuvius Eruption
  • the Great Plague

I, Titus, am Emperor of Rome! I am quite brilliant; I know Latin and Greek and am a sharp military commander. I am generally liked by the people and marry Marcia Furnilla, and then have a relationship with Bernice, the Queen of Judaea. But my reign was plagued with sorrow and destruction. Primo, Mons Vesuvius erupit, vastans urbes Pompeii et Herculanii. Then, a fire in Rome broke out for three days straight; after that, a great plague overcame my people. I tried to offer comfort and relief to my people, but I just could not take it anymore. So, I fled to my family’s villa in the Sabine Hills and died there. What I am most proud of is completing the Flavian Amphitheatre, otherwise known as the Coliseum.


Titus Flavius Vespasianus had two sons, Titus and Domitian. Together, they are known as the Flavian emperors. Let’s learn more about them, and you determine if you’d consider them a good emperor or a bad emperor.

Let’s visualize my reign!

Arch of Titus

Erected by his younger brother, Domitian, in 81 CE, it commemorates his victory and his father, Vespasian’s, victory in the Jewish Wars. It is located in the Palatine Hill in the Roman Forum on the Via Sacra.

Coliseum

Vespasian began construction on this wonder around 70 CE. It took nearly ten years to complete and was opened by Titus around 80 CE. With its commencement, Titus held 100 days of games for the Roman people. Today, it is one of the seven wonders of the world.

Pompeii

In August of 79 CE, Mt. Vesuvius, located in the Gulf of Naples, Italy, erupted. Its volcanic smoke and ash plumed over the neighboring city of Pompeii, suffocating its residents. Within 24 hours, all but 2,000 residents had perished as a result of the eruption. The volcanic ash preserved the ruins of the city, making it a remarkable archaeological and tourist site today.

“A cloud, from which mountain was uncertain, at this distance (but it was found afterwards to come from Mount Vesuvius), was ascending, the appearance of which I cannot give you a more exact description of than by likening it to that of a pine tree, for it shot up to a great height in the form of a very tall trunk, which spread itself out at the top into a sort of branches.” (Pliny the Elder, Letters to Tacitus, LXV)

Herculaneum

Located at the western base of Mt. Vesuvius, Herculaneum was engulfed by Mt. Vesuvius’s gaseous fumes and ravaging mud during its eruption. The town was completely destroyed, although there were more survivors than Pompeii, since Herculaneum was located farther away than Pompeii from the volcano. It is also a well-preserved town and a popular tourist destination.
