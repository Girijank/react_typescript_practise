Art and Architecture of the Roman Provinces

We need to be smart about our plan for Rome’s dominance. One of the ways that Rome had developed its power was by spreading its master building plan into other countries and provinces across the classical world! Brilliant!

'All buildings must be executed in such a way as to take account of durability, utility, and beauty.'
— Vitruvius, On Architecture, book I, Ch. III

The Roman army, along with allied troops from the provinces, would build roads, buildings, statues, and monuments reflective of the Roman culture in the annexed areas. In this way, the presence and image of Rome would be impressed upon these foreign territories. As a result, provinces would now contain Roman architecture, artwork, and infrastructure throughout. Rome was taking over the world!

Grab your passport, and let’s go around the world to visit the provinces of the Roman Empire to see how the Roman emperors erected architectural masterpieces everywhere!

Architecture in Roman Provinces

France + Spain

Pont du Gard near Nimes, Gaul

This large bridge-aqueduct was constructed in 19 BCE by Agrippa to carry water throughout Gaul (modern day France).

Maison Carrée at Nimes, Gaul

This temple was built in Gaul in 16 BCE and dedicated to Caesar Augustus.

Tarragona Amphitheatre, Hispania

The amphitheatre used for gladiatorial games near the Mediterranean Sea in Hispania was built in the 2nd century CE.

Roman Walls in Lugo Hispania

This defensive wall was built to defend the barbarian threat in the 3rd c CE.

Africa

Basilica of Volubilis, Moracco

It was an administrative Roman province used as a fruitful trading post in Northern Africa. Today, this basilica, built in the 3th century CE, is an UNESCO world heritage site.

Theatre of Alexandria, Egypt

This theatre was constructed in the 2nd or 3rd century CE and is full of mosaics. It was built for religious celebrations.

Temple of Dougga, Tunisia

Constructed in 166 CE, this massive temple was dedicated to Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva.

Great Britain

Housesteads Roman Fort, Northumberland, England

This 2.2-hectare fort, built in stone around 124 CE as an auxiliary fort to Hadrian’s Wall, housed a military garrison of nearly 1,000 men—infantry and calvary of conquered people.

Roman Villa at Fishbourne Roman Palace, West Sussex

Constructed in 70 CE, this is a full Roman palace with floor mosaics.

Roman Baths in Somerset, England

In 40–70 CE, the Roman baths were built in the Roman settlement of Aquae Sulis.
