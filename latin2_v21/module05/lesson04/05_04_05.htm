Vita Domitiani

Reign: 81 CE–96 CE. I am known for:

  • being known as the “bald Nero”
  • the writer Juvenal’s Satires about Domitian
  • the Reign of Terror

I, Domitian, have become Emperor of Rome and I am loving it! You see, I am mean, ruthless, conniving, and terrifying. I disliked the Senate and refused to work with them. I killed my niece Julia, Vestal Maidens, foreigners, and anyone who spoke out against me. A reign of terror! Oh, how I loved being the emperor! I censored aliquem scriptorem qui mala de me scripsit. I detested actors, artists, charlatans, sycophants, and women, to name a few. I hated Jewish people, and I suppressed anyone who opposed me. I was brutal and probably one of the most savage emperors of all time. I even had Romans call me dominus et deus—“lord and god.” I was, though, successful in my campaigns in Germany and Dacia. Finally, my reign came to an end when my own wife had me killed Argh!

Practice your participle usage in this passage by the Roman author, Suetonius, who wrote The Lives of the 12 Caesars, an account of the 12 Caesars of Rome. This passage is from his book on Caesar Domitian. You’ll be able to understand Domitian’s character from this reading.

Inter initia principatus cotidie secretum sibi horarum sumere solebat nec quicquam (anything) amplius quam muscas (flies) captare ac stilo praeacuto configere, ut cuidam interroganti, essetne (if there was) quis intus cum Caesare, non absurde responsum sit (there was) a Vibio Crispo, ne muscam quidem.

In the beginning of his princeps in hiding, he was accustomed to spending hours by himself and not anything more than capturing flies or stabbing them with a very sharp stylus. As for anyone asking, if there was anyone inside with Caesar, there would was an absurd answer by Vibius Crispus, not a fly even.

Deinde uxorem Domitiam, ex qua in secundo suo consulatu filium tulerat alteroque anno quam imperium adeptus est amisit, consalutavit Augustam.

Then, he greeted his wife Domitia as Augusta, from whom in his second consulship he had borne a son and he lost him in the second year which he obtained emperorship.

Eandem Paridis histrionis amore deperditam repudiavit intraque breve tempus impatiens discidii quasi efflagitante (desiring) populo reduxit. (lines 347–351)

He repudiated this same woman having been desperate from the love of the actor Paris, and within a short time, he being impatient of leaving, remarried her as if because of the desiring people.
