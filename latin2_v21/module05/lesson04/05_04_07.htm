Ablative Absolute in Latin

Now, let’s turn to recognizing the Ablative Absolute in Latin. They are really easy to spot! Keep to your translation guidelines from the previous page and you’ll be absolutely golden.

There are two different sentence types which call for an Ablative Absolute. Select the tabs to see how the Ablative Absolute works!

Ablative Absolute

 

  • Translate: "with __(noun/pronoun/adjective)__(translation of participle)
    • Ablative Absolute with perfect Passive participle: “with the noun having been __(verb)___ed"
    • Ablative Absolute with present Active participle: “with the noun (verbing)”
    • Ablative Absolute with future Active participle: “with the noun (about to verb)”
    • Example: “With the army having been defeated”; “with the mother smiling”; “with the tutor about to teach”
Translation Examples
Latin Sentence English Translation Ablative Absolute Identification
Oppidis nostris ab hostibus captis, bellum geremus. With our towns having been seized by the enemy, we will wage war. Oppidis nostris captis= Ablative plural neuter from “oppidum, oppidi n. + PPP of capio, capere, cepi, captus,-a,-um = to seize.” The ab hostibus is the Ablative of Agent because captis is in the Passive Voice. See how “captis” is translated as “having been seized” in the Ablative Absolute.
Lupa dentem amisura, oves feliciores videtur. With the wolf about to lose a tooth the sheep seem happier. Lupa amisura = Ablative singular feminine. The dentem in the sentence is the direct object of the amisura which is the future Active participle of “amitto, amittere, amisi, amisurus,-a,-um = to lose.” See how “amisura” is translated as “about to lose” in the Ablative Absolute.

Diana's Directive: Hunting for the Ablative Absolute

To identify the Ablative Absolute look for the following:

  1. It must contain a noun/pronoun/adjective in the Ablative Case, or the PPP can stand alone in a substantive adjectival form.
  2. Most of the time, it will also contain a perfect Passive participle (4th principal part of the verb) in the Ablative Case. However, it can also have a present Active participle or future Active participle. The participle will match the noun/pronoun/adjective in the Ablative Case, number, and gender.
  3. Look out for a noun/pronoun/adjective and participle both written with Ablative endings.
  4. The Ablative Absolute often begins a Latin sentence; but no matter in what position you may find it, it is set off by commas.
  5. The present Active participle will use regular 3rd Declension endings when inside of an Ablative Absolute.
  6. The Ablative Absolute clause can contain the Genitive, Dative, Accusative, or Ablative Cases inside of it, depending on what is needed to be stated in the sentence. So, the clue to translating these other cases within the Ablative Absolute is to:
    • Find the Ablative Absolute participial phrase and have it translated.
    • Then, translate the other cases as they best fit into the Ablative Absolute clause.
