The Ablative Absolute

We are now going to move to applying our participles in a brand-new form known as the Ablative Absolute. You’ll absolutely love it!

Before we dive into learning what the Ablative Absolute means, review your adjective and verb forms.

The Ablative Absolute is a dependent clause that will never be the subject of the sentence. It is also not grammatically connected to the main sentence. It is easy to recognize because it is normally written at the beginning of a sentence.

Let’s first take a look at how you would identify an Ablative Absolute phrase in English. We have bracketed off the Ablative Absolute in the sentence so you can begin to see it clearly. For now, we will translate the Ablative Absolute, using the word “with” before the noun.

Nota bene:

The subject of the Ablative Absolute participial phrase will never be the subject of the main sentence.

[With Julius Caesar having been killed,] the conspirators fled Rome.

Ablative Absolute phrase: With Julius Caesar having been killed,

See how the subject of the main sentence, “conspirators,” is not the subject of the Ablative Absolute participial phrase.

More examples of the Ablative Absolute

[With Rome having been consumed by a fire,] Nero was able to build his new home, the Domus Aurea.
  • Ablative Absolute phrase: With Rome having been consumed by a fire,
  • See how the subject of the main sentence, “Nero,” is not the subject of the Ablative Absolute participial phrase.
[With the enemy having been captured,] the commander celebrated a victory.
  • Ablative Absolute phrase: With the enemy having been captured,
  • See how the subject of the main sentence, "commander," is not the subject of the Ablative Absolute participial phrase.
[With the light dimming,] the children went to sleep.
  • Ablative Absolute phrase: With the light dimming,
  • See how the subject of the main sentence, “the children,” is not the subject of the Ablative Absolute participial phrase.
[With the wolf planning an evil deed,] the sheep seemed very nervous.
  • Ablative Absolute phrase: With the light dimming,
  • See how the subject of the main sentence, “the sheep,” is not the subject of the Ablative Absolute participial phrase.
