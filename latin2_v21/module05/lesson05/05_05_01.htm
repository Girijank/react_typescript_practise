Ancient Eco-Warrior

Empires need four major items for success in long-lasting survival: land, people, resources, and control. These provide economic growth and the financial stability needed to expand an empire. To achieve its success, the Roman Empire continuously waged wars to increase its land mass, subjugated people and towns, augmented its army, gained revenue, and Romanized the world.

With its expanding land mass, Rome’s economic vitality increased in large part due to its ability to now trade the goods of its newly acquired provinces and to exploit the natural resources of these new lands for Rome’s own needs. But this was not a good thing for the environment, since the exploitation of land and natural resources included:

  • hunting and gathering in uncultivated lands to almost extinction of animals and food supply
  • creating colonies and cities for the sole purpose of exploiting its raw resources
  • farming, mining, and quarrying to harness the power of wind and water for irrigation
  • deforesting of timber for structural building, ship building, and fuel supply
  • restructuring of natural water sources, including drainage of rivers and lakes, and restructuring of mountain ranges for expansion of towns

Natural Resources

The Romans exploited natural resources (land, mineral and ores, agricultural and water sources) from each of the provinces they controlled. They developed technology specifically for extraction and used human labor for the extraction. The Romans overmined salt from the province of Asia, ore from the quarries of Egypt, grain, vegetables and olives of the Maghreb region of North Africa, and gold from gold mines of northern Spain to help support Rome’s elite troops and its widespread empire.

These acts caused great damage and destruction to the provinces' environment and ecology. For example, Rome’s overexploitation of timber caused such widespread deforestation that the imperial lands experienced heavy erosion. This decreased the quality of the soil, causing the food supply to dwindle. Historians believe this might have been one of the main reasons for the fall of the Roman Empire. If only the Romans had realized that protecting the environment, instead of destroying it, could have saved their civilization!

Sample Letter: “Save our Forests!”

Dear Emperor Otho,

Salve. I believe your time as emperor will be brief, so let me get straight to the point. If you want to truly make a name for yourself this year, be the emperor who protects our forests and the one who is a champion for those of us who care about our lands.

I live in the Roman province of northern Gaul, and I know that our forest trees are being cut down to be used for building Rome’s housing structures, building our massive war fleets, and heating Rome’s baths and public facilities. I understand that wood is essential for our mining and smelting purposes so we can have necessary resources such as salt, iron, copper, and gold… and for padding the pockets of the nobles.

However, I must tell you that Rome’s excessive mining production is causing devastation to our forests. In fact, since wood is constantly needed for the furnaces and smelters at each mining area, our forests are disappearing at alarming rates. Not only is this happening here in northern Gaul, but my friends in other provinces tell me their forests are nearly depleted as well. The clearing of our land has decreased the nutrients of our topsoil so much that we are now concerned that our harvesting yields will actually diminish because our land is becoming increasingly infertile.

Because of deforestation, I am afraid that we will see more floods, causing our coastlines, rivers, and water supplies to change. I am afraid that our birds and forest animals will lose their homes, and we will run out of food for future generations. I am afraid that our wonderful Roman Empire will crumble like those before it, all because we did not take care of our forests. Other civilizations have seen their lands destroyed by deforestation. Remember the wise Plato in the 5th century BCE even said, “The loss of timber had denuded the hills and plains surrounding Athens and caused massive soil erosion.” Let’s not be like the Greeks and witness our civilization whittle away because of deforestation! How about when you cut down a tree, you plant one in its place? Let’s together save our trees and save Rome, Otho!

Here are three ways, Otho, I believe you can be of immediate help:

  • Te Imperatore in LXIX C.E., legem servantem silvas nostras statua! (With you as emperor in 69 CE, establish a law protecting our forests!)
  • Oppidis conditis, avibus in arboribus benignitatem monstrare incolis oportet. (After the towns have been established, it is necessary for the inhabitants to show kindness to the birds in the trees.)
  • Es ferox, Otho, et es imperator, qui futuro nostro spem perpetuam creaturus est. (Be courageous, Otho, and be the emperor, who is about to create lasting hope for our future.

Gratias Imperatori,

Lucilius

Martius I, LXIX C.E.
