Perfect Passive Participle (PPP)

Now, let’s dig deep into our next participial form! You’ll love this one. Hint: You already know it!!

  • Latin Form: It is the 4th principal part of the verb.
  • Declined: It is declined like the adjective magnus, -a, -um.
  • Agreement: It agrees, like a regular adjective, with the noun or pronoun it modifies in case, number, and gender.
    • example: oppidum occupatum = the town having been seized = Nominative (or Accusative) singular neuter

Nota bene:

Do you recall that you have already been using the 4th principal part of the verb for the Perfect Passive tense, the Pluperfect Passive tense, and the Future Perfect Passive tense? So, you know it very well! The only thing new here is the fact that you will have the PPP standing alone in a sentence. Don’t worry; it likes being alone!

We must treat participles with care and understand how they function in a sentence. Participles can function as adjectives or as nouns in a sentence. Let’s see how this works.

Perfect Passive Participle as Adjectives

When functioning as an adjective, the participle will describe the noun. You will decide whether to translate the participle before the noun it modifies or after the noun it modifies. You will choose the correct translation based upon what makes best sense in the sentence.

Example : The girl’s heart, having been broken after losing the baking contest, became full again when she ate a delicious cookie.

Participle : having been broken

Participle Explanation : Which heart? The heart which was broken. Verbal Adjective Nota bene: See how the broken is both a verb action, while at the same time describing the girl’s heart.

The soldier, having been chosen by the leader, left. Having been chosen is the participle, modifying “the soldier.” The soldier, having been chosen = subject of the sentence.
Having not been dressed, Diana changed Actaeon into a deer. Having not been dressed is the participle, modifying “Diana.” Having not been dressed, Diana = subject of the sentence. It is just appearing before her name for emphasis.
