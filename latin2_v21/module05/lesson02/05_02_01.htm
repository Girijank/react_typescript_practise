The Roman Empire

The Roman Empire had begun! Emperors ruled Rome from the reign of Augustus until the fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 CE. The emperor had auctoritas and the ability to control the senatus populusque Romanus on his own. The Senate was respected, but in name only and no longer held much power over the state in the way it had during the Roman Republic. In fact, the Senate had given Augustus and his successors (unknowingly) imperium maiusover the provincial governor, who had tribunica potestas and the authority to create, pass, and veto laws. As a result, Augustus was able to bring stability and peace to Rome. However, Rome was now in the hands of an emperor and would remain so for centuries to come.

I am Emperor!

Augustus would appoint a member of his extended family to be his successor as emperor. This would carry on, for the most part, as the tradition for how an emperor would be would be chosen thereafter. The new emperors would assume the title of Caesar Augustus as their common name—to show their imperial ties to Augustus—and retain his power and right to rule.

As you might have guessed, this type of familial passing of power at times was a nightmare for Rome. Many emperors were consumed by their power and ruled Rome in a tyrannical fashion.
