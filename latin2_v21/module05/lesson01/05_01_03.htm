Participles + Present Active Participle

Get ready for a new grammatical form! It is called a participle. A participle is a verbal adjective. It is a half-verb, half-adjective, and it modifies a noun in a sentence.

Before you get to know participles, review your Verb Guide and Noun/Adjective/Pronoun Guide for useful terms related to participle formation.You can also review verbs and their conjugations in your Toolbox.

Recognizing Participles

Think of a participle like the mythological centaur creature. A centaur is half man/ half horse, so it is a hybrid animal. Well, a participle is a hybrid part of speech—not quite a verb—not quite an adjective. It is a verbal adjective!

Participles will: Participles will not:
  • be easy and fun to spot in a sentence
  • decline like a noun/adjective
  • have a verb person (1st,2nd,3rd)
  • be conjugated

There are three Latin tenses for participles:

  • Present Active
  • Future Active and Passive (Nota bene: Future Passive participle is not covered in this course.)
  • Perfect Passive

Participle Examples Participle Tense
Crying; while crying Present Active
About to cry Future Active
About to be eaten Future Passive
Having been eaten; eaten Perfect Passive

Present Active Participles in English

  • The present Active participle denotes action that is happening now.
  • Translate it as “verbing” or “while verbing.”

We must treat participles with care and understand how they function in a sentence. Participles can function as adjectives or as nouns in a sentence. Let’s see how this works.

Present Active Participles as Adjectives Present Active Participles as Nouns
When functioning as an adjective, the participle will describe the noun. You will decide whether to translate the participle before the noun it modifies or after the noun it modifies. You will choose the correct translation based upon what makes the best sense in the sentence. When functioning as a noun, the participle will stand by itself in a sentence. It can be the subject, object of possession, indirect object, direct object, and object of the preposition in sentences.

Example: The bathing bird splashes around in the puddle.

Participle: bathing

Participle Explanation: Which bird? The bathing bird.

Verbal Adjective nota bene: See how bathing is both a verb action while at the same time describing the bird.

Example: Laughing sparks joy in Craig, who normally only likes to read Latin poetry.

Participle: laughing

Participle Explanation: It stands alone in the sentence and is in the form of a participle (verbal-adjective); but since it stands alone, it is functioning as a noun. Think of it in our head to read as: “the act of laughing…” However, in Latin, we do not use participles in this manner.

Present Active Participle Usage

English Sentence Present Active Participle Identification + Explanation Function of Participial Phrase
The shouting man attacked the enemy. Shouting is the participle, modifying “man.” The shouting man = subject of the sentence
Caesar Augustus,while conquering the world, became a provincial governor. While conquering is the participle, modifying “Caesar Augustus.” Caesar Augustus while conquering = subject of the sentence
The roars of the laughing audience disturbed the dramatic actors on stage. Laughing is the participle, modifying “audience.” of the laughing audience = possession
The enemy will seize the men creating boats for the Romans. Creating is the participle, modifying “the men.” the men creating = direct object of the sentence
Corruption was spread amongst the Senate hoping for absolute power. Hoping is the participle, modifying “Senate.” amongst the Senate hoping for = prepositional phrase
