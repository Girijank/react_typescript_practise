I’m a Non-Roman Living in a Roman Province!

When Rome created their provinces, they expected these foreign inhabitants to incorporate Roman standards and customs into their daily lives with a process of assimilation. However, Rome did not always grant the same rights or freedom to its provincial residents as it did to its own citizens. What types of freedom, rights, and upward mobility an inhabitant had depended on what type of province he lived in. There were three types of provinces:

  • The Roman colonies and municipia
  • The Latin colonies and municipia
  • The non-Roman towns, including (a) free towns, allied and non-allied, and (b) tributary or stipendiary towns

That Province Life!

Advantages Disadvantages
The inhabitants of full Roman colonies possessed equal rights with those living in Rome. These individuals were required to incorporate Roman standards and customs into their daily lives as a process of assimilation. Inhabitants within non-Roman towns did not possess Roman citizenship, but they could receive it as a special gift or from one who already had it. The chief burdens on the provinces were both militaristic and financial. The provinces had to constantly supply men to the Roman army, and they were also responsible for heavily taxing their residents so they could pay tribute to the imperial city. The Romans exploited the inhabitants of their provinces by forcing them to become Rome’s major labor force to help expand the empire.

In the provinces:

  • You could rise up in status more easily.
  • As a freedman, you could rise up in politics and help your family do the same!
  • Women held greater status and could work as midwives, shopkeepers, or barmaids and grocers.
  • To appease native tribes to help Rome defend itself, Rome would often bribe native tribes in their provinces with gifts or promise their leaders friendships or allegiances if they cooperated.

The provincial residents were responsible for:

  • Building the infrastructure of new towns
  • Extracting the raw resources
  • Producing agricultural goods for the imperial city
  • Maintaining public streets and access ways

Burdens on the provincial resident

The worst part is that, as a non-citizen, you didn’t have much protection, and so you could not defend yourself legally against any punishments handed to you. You could be executed easily. Non-Roman citizens were often crucified in public or forced to fight to the death in gladiatorial games as their capital punishment.

Did all provinces comply?

Non-Roman towns maintained their own system of government, outside of Roman administration. Thus, many towns did not assimilate. Instead, they operated under their own selected leadership, maintained their own laws, and sustained their own local customs. This would cause conflict between Rome and a province. Here are some examples:

  • It took Rome nearly 200 years to subdue Spain.
  • There were numerous revolts in Jerusalem against Rome. About 1 million Jews had died during Titus’s reign.
  • The Romans were brutal towards those cities that rebelled against becoming a province. They would conduct public executions and plunder them to force them into submission. Remember the Boudica rebellion?
