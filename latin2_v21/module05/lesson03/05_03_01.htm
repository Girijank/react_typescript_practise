Rome and its Provinces

When Augustus and his successors held military campaigns throughout Europe, Asia, and Africa, they would leave members of their military legions behind in the conquered areas to set up colonies. These areas became properties of Rome known as provinces. A peace treaty was signed between the conquered land and Rome, giving Rome control over land boundaries, taxes, and administrative structure. Although each province was self-administered by its local magistrates, the emperor appointed a governor or representative of the emperor to carry out the province’s administrative duties.

A True Empire

The Roman Empire consisted of the city of Rome and all of the cities and countries throughout the Mediterranean world that it had conquered and made into provinces. By the 2nd c. CE, Rome had 50 to 60 million inhabitants living in its provinces and thousands of kilometers of borders to defend, due to its expansion efforts. The Roman Empire was colossal, and it developed an operating manual to which many world Empires to come would follow.

  1. Conquer and control as much land as possible.
  2. Subjugate and exploit the people of the conquered land.
  3. Extract resources from the conquered land to increase Rome’s wealth.
  4. Make the province reliant upon Rome for protection against threats.
  5. Provide urbanization to the province.
  6. Collect revenue, taxes, and subsidies from the province as a money-making income stream.

Co-Dependency

Rome and its provinces had a co-dependent relationship. Rome, of course, was the more dominant figure in this relationship, but let’s learn how each supported one another!

Province:

  • gives soldiers for the army
  • pays taxes to the Empire
  • provides slaves for the Empire’s agricultural supply
  • provides raw resources for imperial expansion, coal, and manufacturing
  • helps spread Roman propaganda

Rome:

  • protects against external and internal threats
  • appoints governors and magistrates to maintain administrative orders
  • makes emperor the face and controlling figure
  • centralized government for maintaining order
  • gains large sums of revenue which finances the Empire’s operations

The largest provinces under Rome were: Africa, Egypt, Gaul, Roman Britain, and Hispania. Take a look at other Roman provinces.
