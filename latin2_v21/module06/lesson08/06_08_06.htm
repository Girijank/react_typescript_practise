Poetry: Vergil, the Aeneid Translation

As a review in Roman poetry and translating, it is only fitting that we end our course with its beginning—the story of Rome’s founding! Let’s translate the first passage from Vergil's famous epic poem, the Aeneid, book I, ll. 1-7, which sings the praises of the beginnings of our glorious Rome!Use your previous knowledge of this story and your Latin Toolbox and Glossary to guide you as you attempt this passage.

Aeneid, Book 1, lines 1-7

Arma virumque cano, Troiae qui primus ab oris (line 1)
Italiam, fato profugus, Laviniaque venit (line 2)

I sing of arms and men, he who, having been exiled by fate, first came from the coast of Troy to Italy, and to the Lavinian shores, (lines 1 and 2)

litora, multum ille et terris iactatus et alto (line 3)
vi superum saevae memorem Iunonis ob iram; (line 4)

he both having been tossed about much by land and by sea by the will of the gods on account of the mindful memory of Juno;

multa quoque et bello passus, dum conderet (he founded) urbem, (line 5)
inferretque (he brought) deos Latio, genus unde Latinum, (line 6)
Albanique patres, atque altae moenia Romae. (line 7)

and also having suffered much in war, until he founded the city, and he brought his gods to Latium, from which the Latin people, the fathers of Albanus, and also the walls of lofty Rome.
