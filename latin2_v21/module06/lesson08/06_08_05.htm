As a review in Roman poetry and translating, let’s translate a passage from Tibullus, Book VI, which includes a love elegy from the female poet, Sulpicia. Sulpicia wrote six love elegies, but none have survived as individually published poems. Instead, the poet Tibullus appended them to his work. In the following poem by Sulpicia, we learn about her love for Cerinthus. For this passage, use your Latin Toolbox and Glossary to guide you.

(Albius Tibullus., 55 BCE–19 BCE.)

Sulpicia: Tibullus, Book VI

Ne (not) tibi sim (may I be), mea lux, aeque iam fervida cura (line 1)

May I not be to you, my light, now a fairly feverish concern

ac videor paucos ante fuisse dies, (line 2)

as I seem to have been before these few days.

si quicquam tota commisi stulta iuventa, (line 3)

If I committed anything in all of my foolish youth

cuius me fatear (I confess) paenituisse magis, (line 4)

of which I confess that I have regretted more

hesterna quam te solum quod noctere liqui, (line 5)

than because I have left you alone last night,

ardorem cupiens dissimulare meum, (line 6)

wishing to hide my passion.
