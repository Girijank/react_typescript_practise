Viva Roma!

Now let’s recall our poetry and oration lessons to celebrate this art form, as I most thoroughly do on Mt. Olympus!

Many Roman poets and orators often conflicted in their viewpoints. Although they contributed to the artistic palate within Rome, they often found themselves at conflict with what they wrote. Catullus was not a fan of Julius Caesar or the corruption of the Roman Republic. Vergil held both pro and anti-sentiments about the Augustan program and wanted his greatest work, the Aeneid, to be burned. Ovid was exiled from Rome because of his love poems. Cicero, a once staunch supporter of the Republic, was later killed by one of the Republic’s greatest leaders.

Their works, though, lived on generations afterwards. Renaissance writers loved Roman elegy. Students today love reading the stories of Ovid’s Metamorphoses. We write poems and songs today inspired by the works of the Roman poets. As you continue on in your studies, remember that these poets and orators helped pave the way for so many literary works of our age, and they contributed greatly to our modern society.

We hope that the Romans have inspired you to be a creative thinker and writer and that you continue to study Latin well into your future, so Latin can continue to inspire you!
