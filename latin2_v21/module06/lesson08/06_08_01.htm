Becoming a Great Orator

Wow! We’ve read some wonderful speeches and poetry in this lesson. If I could read poetry, listen to music, and write great stories all day, I would be truly satisfied!

Have you ever wanted to become a great speech writer? Well, congratulations! You have now learned the parts of a great speech and have been introduced to some of history's greatest speakers as mentors.

Moving forth, to craft a great speech, all you need to do is follow the techniques and style of both the Roman and modern speeches you have read in these lessons. When you craft your next speech, go through this checklist to see if you have hit all of these points to improve your own presentation skills:

Oration Skills Checklist:

Checklist 1

A Great Speech

Have you ever heard a speech that rocked you? It probably included some Roman oratorical devices.

Apollo’s Aim:

Do you think Taylor Swift will think about us Romans when giving her acceptance speech for yet another Grammy?

Checklist 2

Substance

Meat and potatoes. The speech has to deliver great content.

Apollo’s Aim:

If we make that a mincemeat pie, here comes my best speech ever!

Checklist 3

Impact

Carefully build up your speech to have climatic points that your audience will forever remember.

Apollo’s Aim:

Oh, ok, now I see—don’t shout out the entire speech. Build it up, Apollo, take your time.

Checklist 4

Delivery and Style

Be persuasive and utilize the speech constructs of Roman oration.

Apollo’s Aim:

Exordium, Narratio, Propositio, Confirmatio, Refutatio, Peroratio. Whew, they all sound the same! I've got to brush up on my Latin vocab!

Checklist 5

Have you memorized your speech?

The Romans would memorize their entire speeches for greatest effect.

Apollo’s Aim:

Take one deep breath and ready, set, go—the entire speech—no notes! Roman brains were no joke!

Checklist 6

Have you practiced your speech in front of a live audience?

Roman schoolchildren were expected to memorize and then practice their speeches in front of their classes.

Apollo’s Aim:

Now who around this Mt. Olympus is going to listen to my speech—Juno? Diana? Mars? Probably not—they always say they’re too busy.
