Julius Caesar’s Oratorical Skills

Julius Caesar was a brilliant orator who had learned the skill of oration in his education. This speech excerpt comes from Caesar’s speech in defense of the Bithynians, which was probably delivered in court in c. 71 BCE, when Caesar was military tribune, early in his career.

Vel pro hospitio regis Nicomedis vel pro horum necessitate quorum res agitur, refugere hoc munus, (line 1)
M. Iunce, non potui. Nam neque hominum morte memoria deleri debet quin a proximis retineatur, (line 2)
neque clientes sine summa infamia deseri possunt, (line 3)
quibus etiam a propinquis nostris opem ferre instituimus. (line 4)

Either on behalf of my guest, friendship with the king Nicomedis or on behalf of the relationship of these men whose case is on trial, I was not able to refuse this duty, (line 1)

Marcus Iuncus. For neither the memory of the men ought to not be destroyed in death without being retained by those nearest them, (line 2)

nor are the clients able to be deserted without an infamous scandal, (line 3)

to whom we planned to bring aid even away from our own relatives. (line 4)

Carpe a, b, c, diem!

In line 1, name the Latin idiom

Res agitur

In line 1, what is the case, number, and gender of horum?

Genitive, plural, masculine

In line 3, what is the form of deseri?

Present passive infinitive

In line 4, what is the case, person, and number of the relative pronoun quibus? What is its antecedent?

Dative, plural, masculine. Antecedent = clientes
