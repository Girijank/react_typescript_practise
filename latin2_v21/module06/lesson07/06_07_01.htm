The Power of Oration

Be inspired to speak words that inspire a generation! Get ready to learn about the great spokespeople of the Roman Republic and Empire and perhaps learn from their spoken word legacy.

Think of the best public speakers you have watched on video or listened to on the radio. Do they inspire you when they speak? Why are their words so powerful? In Rome, students were trained to be public speakers, or orators. Being a great orator was a prized skill, as it helped you move up the cursus honorum. If you spoke well, you could be a changemaker in the Senate, with “fans” around town supporting your opinions. On the other hand, being well-spoken and outspoken also had the potential at times to make you public enemy number one.

The Romans modeled their oratory methodologies after the Greeks, who were specifically trained at Athens and Rhodes in oratory technique. By the 1st century BCE, Rome had fully embraced the art of rhetoric. The primary purpose of oration was (and still is) to persuade the audience to your point of view. For Romans, one’s effectiveness as a public speaker directly affected public and political life. If an individual was good at oral debates in the Senate, he was allowed to speak earlier than others in debates, and his speaking skills would bolster his influence amongst senators.

Recipe for a Great Orator:

  1. A great speech:

    A masterfully constructed speech can move an audience.

  2. Substance:

    A speech must have substance, material that means something to its audience, while promoting idealism.

  3. Impact:

    A persuasive speech impacts the hearts and minds of those hearing or reading it.

  4. Delivery and style:

    The orator must know how to deliver the speech with personal style for maximum impact on the audience.
