Cicero

Rome’s leading rhetorician and orator was a “novus homo” by the name of Marcus Tullius Cicero. He was a lawyer, senator, writer, and trained orator. Born in 106 BCE, he had lived through the first and second Civil Wars, the unraveling of the Republic, and he lived one year past the assassination of Julius Caesar. He rose up the cursus honorum to become consul of Rome. He wrote many importantly transformational speeches and books during his lifetime.

Translate this passage to learn more about Cicero:

Cicero orator famosus in tempore Rei Publicae erat.
Multos libros de philosophia atque amicis suis litteras, quae superstant, scripsit.
Olim verba defendere virum nomine Sexti Roscii Amerini, qui morte patris sui accusatus est, dedit.
Romani putaverunt patricidiam esse crimen horribillimum et ei poenam certam dederunt.
Vir, qui patrem necat, in sacculo cum simio caneque serpenteque pullaque ponitur et tum sacculum in mare iacitur.
Iudices Roscium noxium iudicaverunt propter defensum forte Ciceronis quod etiam nunc legere possumus.
Cicero amator fortis Rei Publicae legumque fuit et cum Pompeio contra Caesarem pugnavit.
Marcus Antonius caput manusque Ciceronis removit et eos in telum ut poena posuit.

Cicero was a famous Roman orator in the time of the Republic.

He wrote many books on philosophy and also many letters (which survive) to his friends.

He once gave a speech to defend a man named Sextus Roscius Amerinus who was accused of the murder of his father.

The Romans thought patricide was a most horrible crime, and they gave it a special punishment.

A man who kills his father is placed in a sack with a monkey, dog, snake, and chicken, and then the sack is thrown into the sea.

The judges did not find guilt for Roscius because of Cicero’s strong defense, which we are able to read even now.

Cicero was a strong lover of the Republic and of the laws, and he fought with Pompey against Caesar.

Marc Antony cut off Cicero’s head and hands and placed them on a spike as punishment.
