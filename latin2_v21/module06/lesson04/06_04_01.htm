War and Poetry

Love and War? Yes, the two do coexist. Some of our best Latin poetry was formed as a result of the poet’s sentiments about the wars in which Rome fought!

What type of poetry would you write if you lived through three Civil Wars, the assassination of Julius Caesar, the end of the Roman Republic, the rise of the sole leader, Augustus, and the rise of the new Roman Empire? Can you imagine the material you could rely on?

War helped shape the poetry of the Roman Republic and Empire. This was true, and Roman poets, like many artists before and after, created works of social commentary on the times in which they lived. The Civil Wars and the destruction of the Roman Republic had disrupted the social fabric and the principles of social equity, virtue, and freedom upon which Rome had operated. The Roman poets who lived through these troubling times took to their papyri to provide their own personal commentaries about the downfall of the Roman Republic.

But poets had to be careful not to outright present Rome as the corrupt, morally decaying city that it had become. This was especially true for those who were commissioned by Augustus to write about the restored glory of the city. When you read Latin poetry, imagine the difficulties poets must have had to both produce authentic artwork, while still being loyal to their patron.

Our Roman poets, Catullus, Horace, Vergil, Propertius, and Tibullus, all wrote through this double-edged lens. For example, Catullus's Carmina constantly criticizes the moral decay of the Roman politician. He even calls Julius Caesar “pathico Caesari.” Catullus himself tried to stitch together a moral code for Roman society throughout his poems. He attempted to get people to turn to poetry as an escape from the heavy burden of the Civil War!
