Summing up the Indirect Statement

Now that you have the indirect statements in your pocket, let’s bring it all together!

Fundamentals

Three Parts of an Indirect Statement

✔ Main “sensory” verb: is within the independent clause.

  • “Sensory verbs” are verbs of thinking, saying, telling, obeying, knowing, perceiving, showing, hearing (or anything that can take place in one’s head).Those type of verbs can introduce an indirect statement clause. We use these sensory verbs in the independent clause to introduce an indirect statement.

✔ Accusative subject: begins the Accusative-infinitive (dependent) clause.

  • The dependent clause in an indirect statement is called an Accusative-infinitive clause. The word functioning as the subject within the Accusative-infinitive clause is in the Accusative case!
  • Nota bene: The “Accusative-subject” of the Accusative-infinitive clause is normally written right after the main sensory verb.

✔ Infinitive verb: is at the end of the entire sentence, and it is part of the dependent clause.

  • The verb within the Accusative-infinitive clause will always be in an infinitive form! However, you will not translate it as an infinitive; instead you’ll translate this like a normally conjugated verb, with its tense dependent on the tense of the main verb.
  • Present infinitives occur at the same time as the action of the main verb.
  • Perfect infinitives occur at the time before the action of the main verb occurs.
  • Future infinitives occur at the time after the action of the main verb occurs.
