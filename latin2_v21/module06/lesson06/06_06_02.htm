Speaking Practice

Now that you know many of Rome’s most famous poets and who financed their works, we have chosen two poets who would be so pleased to know that you are hearing their words come to life. Pretend you are at one of their recitals, listening to them deliver one of their famous poems. Then, record yourself repeating them to hear and compare your pronunciation. Once you are happy with the way the first poem sounds, give the second a try!

The more you speak Latin out loud, the more comfortable you will feel, pronouncing and discussing the vocabulary and phrases you learn with your instructor. Recite your Latin poetry loudly and with pride!

Catullus

Miser Catulle, desinas ineptire,

Wretched Catullus, may you stop making a fool of yourself,

et quod vides perisse perditum ducas.

and that which you see you see has been lost may you lead to perdition.

fulsere quondam candidi tibi soles,

once splendid suns shone bright for you,

cum ventitabas quo puella ducebat

when you kept coming where your girl led

amata nobis quantum amabitur nulla.

no lover will be loved as much than by me.

Ovid

Me miserum! lacrimas male continet oraque dextra

Wretched me! She poorly keeps in check her tears and

protegit ingenuas picta rubore genas.

she covers up with her right hand her natural cheeks painted with blush.

sustinet antiquos gremio spectatque capillos,

She holds up and observes her former hair in her lap,

ei mihi, non illo munera digna loco!

For me indeed, not a worthy tribute for that place!

Collige cum vultu mentem! reparabile damnum est.

Compose yourself with your countenance! The loss is reparable.

postmodo nativa conspiciere coma.

A little later you will be seen with your natural hair.
