Indirect Statement Formula—Text Version

Announcer: Indirect statements occur when a speaker reports what someone else has said, done, or thought.

They don't relay information; they tell you about that information.

[On screen text: Sarah said that Carlos is in first place. I think that you're the winner of the contest. Mrs. Collins knows that our team will probably be last.]

In Latin, indirect statements will always follow a formula, making long sentences easier to recognize and translate.

Indirect Statement Formula

A Latin sentence needs three parts to be considered an indirect statement:

First, a main clause using a sensory verb.

Then, a subject for the indirect statement in the accusative case.

Last, an infinitive acting as the verb in the indirect statement.

[On screen:

  1. Main Sensory Verb
  2. Accusative Subject
  3. Infinitive Verb

     They are being emphasized as the announcer mentions them.]

Let's look at the sentence: "Puella dicit puerum eam amare."

The main clause is indicated by the sensory verb "dicit," or "she says." [On screen: "Dicit" is circled in the sentence "Puella dicit puerum eam amare."]

Test whether the sentence is an indirect statement by using the word "that" in translation following the main verb.

[On screen: "Dicit puerum eam amare." with translation "She says that the boy loves her." "That" is highlighted in the sentence "She says that the boy loves her."] This one translates as: "She says that the boy loves her."

Look at the indirect statement portion of the sentence: puerum eam amare. [On screen text: "Puerem eam amare" is circled in the sentence "Dicit puerum eam amare." "That" is highlighted in sentence translation "She says that the boy loves her."]

Part 2 is the accusative subject of the indirect statement, which follows the sensory verb in the sentence.

In this case "puerum," the boy, is the subject. [On screen text: "Puerum" is circled in the sentence "Dicit puerum eam amare." "The boy" is underlined in the sentence "She says that the boy loves her."]

The infinitive verb, "amare," is part 3 of the indirect statement formula. [On screen text: "Amare" is circled in the sentence "Dicit puerum eam amare.) "Loves her" is underlines in the sentence "She says that the boy loves her."]

Put together, it makes the indirect statement "The boy loves her." [On screen text: "Puerum eam amare" is circled in the sentence "Dicit puerum eam amare." "The boy loves her" is underlined in the sentence "She says that the boy loves her."]

Using the formula, we know that the phrase "the boy loves her" is indirectly said by the girl.

That makes the sentence an indirect statement.
