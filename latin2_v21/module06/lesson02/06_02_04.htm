Infinitive Tenses in Indirect Statement

Remember the indirect statement checklist?

✔ The main “sensory” verb: within the independent clause

✔ The Accusative subject: begins the Accusative-infinitive (dependent) clause

✔ The infinitive verb: at the end of the entire sentence, part of the dependent clause

According to your indirect statement formula, we know that the verb within the Accusative-infinitive clause will be in the infinitive form. But how do you translate it?

  • First, Latin only uses three Infinitive tenses for the indirect statement:
    • Present infinitive (Active or Passive):
    • Perfect infinitive (Active or Passive)
    • Future infinitive (Active or Passive)

Check out this video to learn how important the sensory verb-infinitive tense relationship is when translating an indirect statement.

The translation of the infinitive tense depends on the tense of the main verb. Think of the infinitive forms as points on a tense timeline:

If the Infinitive in the Indirect Statement is… Then… Time Relationship
Main “Sensory” Verb versus Infinitive
Present Tense Your main verb tense and infinitive translation align on a verb tense timeline. Same Time
Perfect Tense The translation of your infinitive moves one step to the left of your main verb on your verb tense timeline. Time Before
Future Tense The translation of your infinitive moves one step to the right of your main verb on your verb tense timeline. Time After
