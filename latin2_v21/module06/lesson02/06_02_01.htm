Patron and the Poet

I am the patron god of poets! Ancient poets turned to me for inspiration and dedicated their works to me. Yes, poets had a mortal patron who financed their art, but I am the only one to whom a poet should truly give any credit!

Poetry was mostly a spoken art form, performed and recited at festivals and public readings. Poets needed someone to finance their artwork, since there was no governmental support or institution that paid for poets to produce poems. Therefore, they would turn to a patron, a wealthy individual who provided money, gifts, and the means by which to perform their poems.

Say My Name!

The patron afforded the poet access to groups of people who would appreciate his poetry. A poet’s patron treated his poet-client in the same manner he would treat his other business clients. For this relationship to work, a poet would:

  • greet his patron early in the morning at his house
  • travel with his patron into the city to attend meetings in the forum
  • provide his patron electoral support when needed
  • mention his patron in his works or dedicate poems to the patron

Circle of Maecenas!

Welcome to the M-Circle, an extraordinary circle of poetic geniuses who transformed Roman literature forever!

My name is Maecenas. I am a patron who financed an entire literary movement during the Augustan Age. I formed the “circle of Maecenas” which consisted of the leading literary minds in Rome. This “circle of Maecenas” helped Augustus craft his literary agenda and propaganda. Without my money, clout, and support, we might not have ever had the works of the poets Vergil, Horace, and Propertius. Augusto magnus amicus eram. When Octavian was abroad before becoming emperor, I helped manage Rome in his absence. I then became a trusted advisor to Augustus during his reign. Augustus, through me, would commission these poets to write their poems. The emperor would make a request for a specific poem topic and style, and I would pass it on—but those moody poets would sometimes write what the muses inspired in them, producing love elegies rather than epics.

In Tuo Orbe

The technical name for “creative license” in Roman literature is called a recusatio.

Think of a patron like a sponsor in today’s world. Athletes, social media influencers, and artists have sponsors who provide money and access to these individuals. This is what a Roman patron did, too!
