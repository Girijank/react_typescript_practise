Visualizing Indirect Statements in Latin

There are three key parts to an indirect statement in Latin:

✔ The main “sensory” verb: within the independent clause

✔ The Accusative subject: begins the Accusative-infinitive (dependent) clause

✔ The infinitive verb: at the end of the entire sentence, part of the dependent clause

We’ll use this Latin indirect statement sentence as our base example:

Dicit virum feminam amare. = He says that the man loves the woman.

This might seem strange to you, so let’s break down how this is an indirect statement sentence through our checklist:

Dicit [virum feminam amare.] = He says that the man loves the woman.

  • Dicit = main sentence (independent clause)
  • [virum feminam amare] = (Accusative-infinitive, dependent clause)
Main “Sensory” Verb Dicit
Accusative Subject virum
Infinitive Verb amare

Let's take a look at what each piece does for our indirect statement formula:

Main "Sensory" Verb (Independent Clause)

Remember, “sensory verbs” are verbs of thinking, saying, telling, knowing, perceiving, showing, hearing, or anything that can take place in one’s head. Those type of verbs can introduce an indirect statement clause. We use these sensory verbs in the independent clause to introduce an indirect statement.

Check out our sentence: Dicit virum feminam amare. = He says that the man loves the woman.

“Dicit” comes from dico, dicere, dixi, dictus,-a,-um = to say. It’s a sensory verb! It is also the main verb of the sentence, acting as an independent clause.

The Accusative-Infinitive Clause (Accusative Subject)

The dependent clause in an indirect statement is called an Accusative-infinitive clause. The word functioning as the subject within the Accusative-Infinitive clause is in the Accusative case! Why? Ask the Romans…

Check out our sentence: Dicit [virum feminam amare.] = He says that the man loves the woman.

  • Virum = the man = the subject of the Accusative-infinitive clause which is “the man loves the woman.”
  • Virum is in the Accusative case because the subject of the Accusative-infinitive clause in Latin is written in the Accusative case.
  • Nota bene: The “Accusative-subject” of the Accusative-infinitive clause is normally written right after the main sensory verb.
The Accusative-Infinitive Clause (Infinitive Verb)

The verb within the Accusative-infinitive clause will always be in an infinitive form! However, you will not translate it as an infinitive; instead, you’ll translate this like a normally conjugated verb. Why again? Ask the Romans…

Check out our sentence: Dicit [virum feminam amare.] = He says that the man loves the woman.

  • amare = loves
  • amare = present Active-infinitive. But it is not translated like its infinitive form (“to love”) — remember to drop the "to"!
  • Nota bene: The tense used by the infinitive in translation depends on the tense of the main verb. You will learn how these tenses function in future lessons.

Apollo’s Aim: that

In order to put your translation in good English word order, you will insert the English word “that” before the Accusative-infinitive (dependent) clause.

The word “that” will not appear in the Latin sentence, so it is up to you to add it into your English translation.
