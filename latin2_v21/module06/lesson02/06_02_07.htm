Translating Indirect Statement: Present Tense Main "Sensory" Verb

Let’s break down how to translate an indirect statement when our main “sensory” verb is present tense. You can use these translation steps for indirect statement to guide your process.

Example Translation Steps
Incolae vident [vorticem naves in saxa rumpere].

The inhabitants see that the whirlpool is breaking the ships against the rocks.
  1. Introduce the "sensory" verb vident. The tense of the main verb is present: "The inhabitants see…"
  2. Insert the English word “that.”
  3. Vorticem is the Accusative subject "The inhabitants see that the whirlpool… "
  4. The infinitive rumpere is present tense = same time as the main verb—"The inhabitants see… the whirlpool is breaking…"
  5. 2nd Accusative is naves.
  6. Other cases are = in saxa = against the rocks.

Now let’s look at each time relationship –same time, time before, and time after.

Present Infinitive: Same Time
Main “Sensory” Verb Tense Latin Sentence English Translation Infinitive Tense + Voice
Present Tense Incolae vident vorticem naves rumpere. The inhabitants see that the whirlpool is breaking the ships. Present Active
Present Tense Incolae vident naves ab vortice rumpi. The inhabitants see that the ships are being broken by the whirlpool. Present Passive
Perfect Infinitive: Time Before
Main “Sensory” Verb Tense Latin Sentence English Translation Infinitive Tense + Voice
Present Tense Incolae vident vorticem naves rupisse. The inhabitants see that the whirlpool was breaking the ships. Present Active
Present Tense Incolae vident naves ab vortice ruptas esse. The inhabitants see that the ships were being broken by the whirlpool. Present Passive
Future Infinitive: Time After
Main “Sensory” Verb Tense Latin Sentence English Translation Infinitive Tense + Voice
Present Tense Incolae vident vorticem naves rupturum esse. The inhabitants see that the whirlpool will break the ships. Future Active
Present Tense Incolae vident naves ab vortice ruptum iri. The inhabitants see that the ships will be broken by the whirlpool. Future Passive

Let’s break these examples down, keeping main verb and infinitive tense in mind. Notice how the Accusative–infinitive clause within the indirect statement is bracketed off.

Example 1
Sentence Latin English
Nauta audit [monstrum aves devorare.] The sailor hears that the monster is devouring the birds.
Main independent clause + sensory verb: Nauta audit
audit = present tense		 The sailor hears
Indirect statement clause: monstrum aves devorare the monster is devouring the birds
Accusative-subject monstrum the monster
Accusative D.O. + other cases/adverbs aves = receives the action of devorare the birds
Infinitive tense, voice, and time devorare = present Active infinitive = same time is devouring
Example 2
Sentence Latin English
Viri sentiunt [deos omen ad omnes misisse.] The men realize that the gods have sent an omen to all.
Main independent clause + sensory verb: Viri sentiunt
sentiunt = present tense		 The men realize
Indirect statement clause: deos omen ad omnes misisse the gods have sent an omen to all
Accusative-subject deos the gods
Accusative D.O. + other cases/adverbs omen
other cases: ad omnes = prepositional phrase 		an omen to all.
Infinitive tense, voice, and time misisse = perfect Active infinitive = time before have sent
Example 3
Sentence Latin English
Senator putat [se magnum ducem futurum esse.] The senator thinks that he himself will be a great leader.
Main independent clause + sensory verb: Senator putat
putat = present tense		 The senator thinks
Indirect statement clause: se magnum ducem futurum esse
*See Apollo’s aim regarding “se” in the indirect statement.		 he himself will be a great leader.
Accusative-subject se he himself
Accusative D.O. + other cases/adverbs Accusative D.O. = magnum ducem = receives the action of esse a great leader
Infinitive tense, voice, and time futurum esse = future Active infinitive = time after will be

Ad astra per sententias!

Translate these indirect statement sentences into English. Remember to pay attention to the tense of the infinitive!

Mater et frater vident [liberos suos in herba ludere].

The mother and father see that [their children are playing in the grass].

Tibullus dicit [Deliam cordem suam rumpisse].

Tibullus says that [Delia broke his heart].

Apollo scit [Mercurium ei lyram dedisse].

Apollo knows that [Mercury gave the lyre to him].

Julius Caesar instat [milites suos in Romam iter facturos esse].

Julius Caesar threatens that [his soldiers will march on Rome].

Apollo’s Aim: Using se with Indirect Statement

When the subject of the main clause and the subject of the indirect statement are the same person, the 3rd person reflexive pronoun, “se” is used as the Accusative subject. Translate it as a “he himself/she herself/it itself/they themselves,” depending on the gender of the main clause subject.

Example:
  • Vir scit se vi feminae amari.
  • The man knows that he himself is being loved by the strength of the woman.
