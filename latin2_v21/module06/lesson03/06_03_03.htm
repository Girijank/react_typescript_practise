Past Tense Main “Sensory” Verb in Indirect Statement

Let’s learn how to translate the indirect statement when the main “sensory” verb is in the imperfect or perfect tenses. Before we begin, keep the translation steps for indirect statement by your side.

Present infinitives occur at the same time as the action of the main verb.

Perfect infinitives occur at the time before the action of the main verb occurs.

Future infinitives occur at the time after the action of the main verb occurs.

If the Main “Sensory” Verb is… And the Infinitive is… Then you translate the Infinitive as… Sentence Example Main Verb-Infinitive Relationship
Imperfect or Present Tense Present Tense Active Voice: was verbing Dicebat/Dixit virum feminam amare.

He was saying that the man was loving the woman.

 Same Time
Passive Voice: was being verbed Dicebat feminam ab viro amari.

He was saying that the woman was being loved by the man.
Imperfect or Perfect Tense Perfect Tense Active Voice: had verbed Dicebat/ Dixit virum feminam amavisse.

He says that man had loved the woman.

Time Before
Passive Voice: had been verbed Dicebat/ Dixit feminam ab viro amatam esse.

He was saying that the woman had been loved by the man.
Imperfect or Perfect Tense Future Tense Active Voice:would love Dicebat/Dixit virum feminam amaturum esse.

He was saying that the man would love the woman.

 Time After
Passive Voice: would be loved Dicebat/Dixit feminam ab viro amatumiri.

He says that the woman would be loved by the man.

Nota bene:

Because the Perfect Passive Participle (PPP) acts like an adjective, the perfect Passive infinitive and the future Active infinitive will always match with the accusative subject of the Accusative-infinitive clause in case, number, and gender.
