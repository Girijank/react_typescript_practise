Translating Indirect Statement: Past Tense– Imperfect or Perfect Tense Main "Sensory" Verb

Let’s break down how to translate an indirect statement when our main “sensory” verb is in the perfect tense. You can use these translation steps for indirect statement to guide your process.

Example: Translation Steps
Incolae videbant [vorticem naves in saxa rumpere].

The inhabitants saw that the whirlpool was breaking the ships.
  1. Introduce the "Sensory" verb videbant, the tense of the main verb is imperfect: "The inhabitants saw…"
  2. Insert the English word “that.”
  3. Vorticem is the Accusative subject. "The inhabitants see that the whirlpool… "
  4. The infinitive rumpere is in the present tense - same time as the main verb—"The inhabitants see… the whirlpool was breaking…"
  5. The 2nd Accusative is naves.
  6. The other case is: in saxa = against the rocks.

Now let’s look at each time relationship –same time, time before, and time after.

Present Infinitive: Same Time
Main “Sensory” Verb Tense Latin Sentence English Translation Infinitive Tense + Voice
Present Tense Incolae viderunt vorticem naves rumpere. The inhabitants saw that the whirlpool was breaking the ships. Present Active
Present Tense Incolae viderunt naves ab vortice rumpi. The inhabitants saw that the ships were being broken by the whirlpool. Present Passive
Perfect Infinitive: Time Before
Main “Sensory” Verb Tense Latin Sentence English Translation Infinitive Tense + Voice
Imperfect Tense Incolae videbant vorticem naves rupisse. The inhabitants saw that the whirlpool had broken the ships. Present Active
Imperfect Tense Incolae videbant naves ab vortice ruptas esse. The inhabitants saw that the ships had been broken by the whirlpool. Present Passive
Future Infinitive: Time After
Main “Sensory” Verb Tense Latin Sentence English Translation Infinitive Tense + Voice
Imperfect Tense Incolae videbant vorticem naves rupturum esse. The inhabitants were seeing that the whirlpool would break the ships. Future Active
Present Tense Incolae viderunt naves ab vortice ruptum iri. The inhabitants saw that the ships would be broken by the whirlpool. Future Passive

Let’s break these examples down, keeping main verb and infinitive tense in mind. Notice how the Accusative–infinitive clause within the indirect statement is bracketed off.

Example 1
Sentence Latin English
Nauta audiebat [monstrum aves devorare.] The sailor was hearing that the monster was devouring the birds.
Main independent clause + sensory verb: Nauta audiebat
audiebat = imperfect tense		 The sailor was hearing
Indirect statement clause: monstrum aves devorare the monster was devouring the birds
Accusative-subject monstrum the monster
Accusative D.O. + other cases/adverbs aves = receives the action of devorare the birds
Infinitive tense, voice, and time devorare = present Active infinitive = same time was devouring
Example 2
Sentence Latin English
Viri senserunt [deos omen ad omnes misisse.] The men have realized that the gods had sent an omen to all.
Main independent clause + sensory verb: Viri senserunt
senserunt = perfect tense		 The men have realized
Indirect statement clause: deos omen ad omnes misisse the gods had sent an omen to all
Accusative-subject deos the gods
Accusative D.O. + other cases/adverbs omen
other cases: ad omnes = prepositional phrase 		an omen
Infinitive tense, voice, and time misisse = perfect Active infinitive = time before had sent
Example 3
Sentence Latin English
Senator putabat [se magnum ducem futurum esse.] The senator was thinking that he himself would be a great leader.
Main independent clause + sensory verb: Senator putabat
putabat = imperfect tense		 The senator was thinking
Indirect statement clause: se magnum ducem futurum esse
*See Apollo’s aim regarding “se” in the indirect statement.		 he himself would be a great leader
Accusative-subject se he himself
Accusative D.O. + other cases/adverbs Accusative D.O. = magnum ducem = receives the action of esse a great leader
Infinitive tense, voice, and time futurum esse = future Active infinitive = time after would be
