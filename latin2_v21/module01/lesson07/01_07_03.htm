Adjective Focus

It’s now time for you to focus your attention on adjectives. Even though these might seem familiar to you from your previous Latin studies, I, Juno, Queen of the gods, command you to review these with me. I am trying to make you a Latin guru now and for the future!

Latin has two sets of adjectives:

  • 1st/2nd Declension adjectives: These adjectives are written and declined in the 1st and 2nd Declensions.
    • Example: certus, certa, certum—fixed, sure
    • Utilizes the Genitive singular feminine, minus the -a ending, as the stem for declining
  • 3rd Declension adjectives: These adjectives are written and declined in the 3rd Declension.
    • Example: brevis, breve—short
    • Utilizes the Genitive singular feminine, minus its ending, as the stem for declining

Unlike English adjectives, the Latin adjective is written after the noun.

  • Example: Rex superbus in regno habitat: The haughty king lives in the kingdom.
  • The exception to this rule: If the adjective is an adjective of size or quantity, it is written before the noun.
    • Example:Brevis rex in regno habitat: The short king lives in the kingdom.

Just as with nouns, Latin adjectives use case endings. Thus, you will also have to recognize the stem from which your adjective comes when looking it up in the dictionary. The goal is to be able to decline an adjective fully.

3rd Declension Adjectives

These adjectives have 3rd Declension endings and need to be declined in the 3rd Declension. Recall that the stem of the adjective is found in the feminine form. You will use this stem as the base for all of the case declining.

3rd Declension Adjectives Declining Rules:

  1. -i in the Ablative singular of all genders
  2. -ium in the Genitive plural of all genders
  3. -ia in the Nominative and Accusative plural of the neuter

There are three sets of 3rd Declension adjectives. Let’s look at how they are declined.
