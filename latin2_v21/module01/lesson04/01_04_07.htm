How to Translate the Passive Voice in Sentences

Translating sentences with the Passive Voice will be easy for you to translate, if you follow these translation tips:

  • Translate the Nominative Case = the noun. (If there is no nominative in the sentence, go to the next tip.)
  • Translate the Passive Verb. (Remember how we translate the Passive Tenses.)
  • Translate the Ablative of Agent = by the noun (see the previous lesson).
  • Use your knowledge of the other cases such as the Genitive, Dative, and Prepositional Phrases, and translate those where they best fit in the sentence.

Select each step to see how it is applied to translating a passive sentence in Latin.

Step 1

Let's translate the sentence:

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 1:

Translate frumentum which is the subject of the sentence (the grain).

Step 2

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 2:

Find our verb = portabitur.

Step 3

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 3:

When looking up our ending, we see it is Future Tense Passive Voice 3rd person sg. It translates as “He/she/it will be carried.”

Step 4

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 4:

  • When we have third person endings, it is always important to look for a nominative.
  • Because our verb ending is singular, we also know that if there is a nominative, it must also be singular.
  • We see frumentum meaning “grain” which can be a neuter nominative singular. This is the “it” that “will be carried.”
  • Now we have “The grain will be carried.” 
Step 5

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 5:

  • Because the verb is passive and the action happens to the nominative, there will be no accusative direct object.
  • We do see the accusative villam meaning “farmhouse.” We also see it comes after the preposition “in,” and that preposition means “into” when the noun following has an accusative ending.
  • Now we have “The grain will be carried into the farmhouse.”
Step 6

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 6:

  • Checking the rest of the sentence, we notice the preposition ab. This can mean “away from”’ or “by” and takes the ablative.
  • We see that agricola is masculine and singular, and the -a ending can be ablative. Because “farmer” is a person and our verb is passive, we know that ablative of agent can be used to show “by whom” the action is done.
  • Now we have: “The grain will be carried into the farmhouse by the farmer.”
Step 7

Frumentum in villam ab agricola fesso portabitur.

Step 7:

  • Checking our last word, fesso, shows that it is an adjective. The -o can be dative singular or ablative singular and it can be masculine or neuter.
  • We have a masculine ablative singular noun already that it can modify, so we can put the two together.
  • Now we have our full sentence: “The grain will be carried into the farmhouse by the tired farmer.”

View these example sentences to see how each word is broken down for translation.

Aeneas a Didone amabatur

Nominative Case: Aeneas = Aeneas

Passive Verb = amabantur = was being loved

Ablative of Agent = a Didone= by Dido.

English Translation: Aeneas was being loved by Dido.

Anchises a filio sub terra visitabitur

Nominative Case: Anchises = Anchises

Passive Verb = visitabitur = will be visited

Ablative of Agent = a filio = by his son

Prepositional Phrase = sub terra = below the earth.

English Translation: Anchises will be visited by his son below the earth.

Multi Troiani in mare iacientur

Nominative case: Multi Troiani = Many Trojans

Passive Verb = iacientur = will be hurled

Ablative of Agent = none

Prepositional Phrase = in mare = into the sea

English Translation: Many Trojans will be hurled into the sea.

Oraculum Cumae ab viris fessis consulitur

Nominative Case: Oraculum = The oracle

Genitive Case: = Cumae= of Cumae

Passive Verb = consulitur = is being consulted

Ablative of Agent = ab viris fessis = by the tired men

English Translation: The oracle of Cumae is being consulted by the tired men.
