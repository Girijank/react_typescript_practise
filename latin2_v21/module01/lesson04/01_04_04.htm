Fabula Fabulosa: Juno's Dearest Dido

'speluncam Dido dux et Troianus eandem
devenient. adero et, tua si mihi certa voluntas,
conubio iungam stabili propriamque dicabo.' —Vergil, Aeneid, book IV, 124-127

'Dido and the Trojan leader will come down to the same cave. I will be there, and if there is a certain desire of yours for me, I will join them in a firm marriage, and I will speak on her behalf.

As Queen of the gods, I already knew that Aeneas was predestined to land in Italy and for his descendants to found the city of Rome. But I did everything in my power to prevent that from happening because I also knew that Aeneas’s descendants, the Roman people, would centuries later fight three great wars called the Punic Wars, against my beloved African nation of Carthage. Romani Carthaginem vastabunt. Therefore, when Aeneas landed in Carthage during his journey and fell in love with the beautiful Carthaginian Queen Dido, I tried to make that relationship last so that Aeneas would stay in Carthage forever, and there could never be a Rome.

But Jupiter intervened and sent his son, the messenger god, Mercury, to Aeneas to tell him to abandon my Queen Dido, break her heart, and leave Carthage. Aeneas, the coward that he is, in media nocte navigavit, leaving my Queen Dido devastated. Eo tempore, perpetuam vindictam petere promisi against Aeneas and his descendants, the Roman people. This is why I brought devastation upon the city of Rome during the Punic Wars.

Cogitemus! (Let's think about it!)

How does this story use both mythological and historical accounts to foreshadow the advent of the Punic Wars?

You can read more about my story in the Aeneid, Book 4, by the Roman author Vergil.
