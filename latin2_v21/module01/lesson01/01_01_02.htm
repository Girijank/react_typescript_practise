Fabula Fabulosa (Fabulous Story): Juno and Io

“aut ego fallor aut / ego laedor” (Ovid, Metamorphoses, Book 1, lines 607, 8)

“either I am deceived or I am wounded” (Ovid, Metamorphoses, Book 1, lines 607, 8)

I am Queen of the gods — fabulous, powerful, and majestic. However, I am known for my temper and ire, jealousy, and my watchful eye on my husband, Jupiter. In my opinion, my vengeful personality is justified — just don’t cross me and you’ll be fine! Listen to my story about how I took out revenge on the river nymph Io.

In my beloved Greek land of Argos, my husband, Jupiter, had seized the river nymph, Io, daughter of the river god Inachus. I flew down to Argos to catch my husband, but before I could find him, he covered the land in a thick mist and turned Io into a cow.

Listen to the rest of my story to find out what happened next:

At first, I actually felt compassion for the cow and told Jupiter to give the cow to me. My husband was reluctant to do so because he was afraid that I would find out about his love for Io. I thought it best I keep a close eye on Io, so I gave the cow to Argus of the 100 eyes to watch over her. Argus never let Io out of his one hundred eyes’ sight until one day she wandered off to the bank of her father’s river. She gazed into the river water and finally realized that she was a cow and no longer a maiden. She cried out to her father who was devastated by his daughter’s transformation! Argos found Io, and brought her back to his lair, but Jupiter wanted Io for himself again. Iupiter Mercurium necare Argum demisit. I was furious when Mercury killed Argus. I wanted revenge on my husband. I scooped up Argus’ one hundred eyes and placed them as jewels on the tail feathers of my favorite bird, the peacock. Then, I sent a gadfly to chase Io around everyday all across the earth which drove her crazy. Io trans Europam multos annos erravit. Jupiter had to beg me to stop my vengeful actions, which I eventually did. But I still believe my anger towards Jupiter and Io was justified.
