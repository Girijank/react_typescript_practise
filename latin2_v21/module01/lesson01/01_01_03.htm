Noun/Adjective/Pronoun Guide
Throughout your Latin studies, you will learn each of these forms.
  • Declension: The family in which a noun belongs. There are five Declensions in Latin
  • Declension Endings: The group of endings that belong to a Declension
  • Case: The grammatical function the noun/adjective/pronoun performs in the sentence
  • Declining: The writing of the noun/adjective/pronoun in each of its cases with case endings in singular and plural

Juno's Note: Latin Noun Dictionary Entries

The dictionary entry for a noun will include four things:

(1) Nominative singular form, (2) Genitive singular form or ending, (3) gender, and (4) definition.

The first two forms, in Latin, are provided to help identify and form the noun in all its cases.

(1) Nominative singular form (remains as-is for all Nominative, singular nouns)

(2) Genitive singular form (used to identify the declension)

Examples:

  • puella, puellae f. girl

    (1) puella, (2) puellae, (3) f.= feminine gender, (4) girl = definition; (5) stem = puell- (from Genitive sg.)

  • ager, agri m. field

    (1) ager, (2) agri, (3) m.= masculine gender, (4) field = definition; (5) stem = agr- (from Genitive sg.)

  • nomen, nominis n. name

    (1) nomen, (2) nominis, (3) n.= neuter gender, (4) name= definition; (5) stem = nomin- (from Genitive sg.)
