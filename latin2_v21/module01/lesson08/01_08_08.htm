Future Perfect Tense Passive Voice

Now let’s roll on to the last tense of our Perfect Passive System. It is the Future Perfect Tense Passive Voice.

The leader will have been loved by the soldiers.
Dux a militibus amatus erit.

The safety will have been preserved by the men.
Salus ab viris servata erit.


How do I form Future Perfect Tense Passive Voice?

Remember to follow these simple steps to conjugate verbs in this form:


  1. 1
    Find the 4th Principle Part of the Verb (PPP) amo, amare, amavi, amatus, -a, -um
    laudo, laudare, laudavi, laudatus, -a, -um
    Nota bene: This is the verb part that ends in -us, -a, or -um. The gender (masculine, feminine, or neuter) and number (singular or plural) of the PPP depend upon the gender and number of the subject of the sentence. removeo, removere, removi, remotus, -a, -um
    teneo, tenere, tenui, tentus, -a, -um
  2. 2
    Add the correct person and number of the Future Tense of the verb “sum.” ero, eris, erit, erimus, eritis, erunt
    Nota bene: The “sum” verb must agree with the subject of the sentence in person and number.
  3. 3

    Put it all together and translate in English:

    Future Perfect Tense Passive Voice
    doceo, docere, docui, doctus, -a, -um = to teach

    Singular Plural
    1st doctus, -a, -um ero
    I will have been taught    		 1st docti, -ae, -a erimus
    We will have been taught
    2nd doctus, -a, -um eris
    You (s.) will have been taught    		 2nd docti, -ae, -a eritis
    You (pl.) will have been taught
    3rd doctus, -a, -um erit
    He/She/It will have been taught    		 3rd docti, -ae, -a erunt
    They will have been taught

Sentence Examples:

Latin Sentence English Translation Verb Agreement
Mare crudele a nautis fessis non laudatum erit. The cruel sea will have not been praised by the tired sailors. laudatum erit = 3rd person singular, neuter to agree with mare crudele.
Inter saxum et locum durum iactus ero. I will have been tossed between a rock and a hard place. iactus ero = 1st person singular, masculine and serves as the subject of the sentence. With “I” as masculine, the subject would be a man.
Brevis homo et alta femina ab agricola divi creti erunt. A short man and a tall woman will have been discerned by a rich farmer. creti erunt = 3rd person plural, masculine to agree with the compound subject brevis homo et alta femina

Ad astra per sententias: Sentence Practice

Translate these sentences from Latin to English, then find the person, number, and gender of Future Perfect Tense Passive Verb (FPP).

Feminae et puellae captae erunt.

Women and girls will have been seized.

captae erunt: 3rd person plural feminine to agree with feminae et puellae which is a combined plural and 3rd person feminine

Rex Etruscanus e Roma pulsus erit.

The Etruscan king will have been expelled out of Rome.

pulsus erit: 3rd person singular masculine to agree with rex etruscanus which is 3rd person singular, masculine

Lex certa a rege malo creta erit.

A certain law will have been discerned by the evil king.

creta erit: 3rd person singular feminine to agree with lex certa which is 3rd person singular, feminine

Juno's Note: Future Perfect Tense Passive Voice Translation

No matter the conjugation, in Latin you will conjugate all verbs in the Future Perfect Tense Passive Voice in the same way. In English, the translation will always have “will have been verbed.”
