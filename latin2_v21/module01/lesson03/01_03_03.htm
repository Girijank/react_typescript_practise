Passive Voice

Congrats! You have mastered how to conjugate and translate verbs in the six tenses: Present, Imperfect, Future, Perfect, Pluperfect, and Future Perfect.

Review your Verb Guide to refresh your memory of the terms you should know when dealing with Latin verbs.

Did you know that you have been conjugating and translating verbs in what we call the Active Voice all along? Did you know, there is another voice to learn? It is called the Passive Voice!

Let’s first review what “voice” is:

The voice of the verb tells you whether the subject does the action, or if the subject is receiving the action of the verb.

  • Active Voice: In the Active Voice, the subject does the action (e.g., amas = you love).
  • Passive Voice: In the Passive Voice, the action instead happens to the subject (e.g., amaris = you are being loved).
Active Voice
The man throws the ball.
The farmer loves the woman.
The inhabitants used to cultivate the fields.
The teacher will teach the student.
The pleasant man will see his wife.
Passive Voice
The ball is thrown by the man.
The woman is loved by the farmer.
The fields used to be cultivated by the inhabitants.
The student will be taught by the teacher.
The wife will be seen by the pleasant man.

Latin sentences can either be in the Active Voice or in the Passive Voice.

Any verb tense can have the Active Voice or Passive Voice.

In Latin, the subject is shown by the person and number indicated by the verb ending.

Latin has separate endings for the Passive Voice for each verb tense so that you can easily see the difference of a voice in a sentence, which will make it very easy for you to translate.

Juno's Note: Passive Voice Translation

  • The Passive form is often shown with a “be/being/been” in translation.

    Example: I am being praised; “being” is called an auxiliary verb.

  • The main verb in the passive voice will never have an “-ing” as its ending in English.

    Example:

    • Active: I am praising.
    • Passive: I am being praised. = no “-ING”
