Lucius Tarquinius Superbus Returns

After Tarquinius Superbus had been exiled by the Romans, he sought an alliance with the Etruscan king Lars Porsenna. Remember that the Tarquin kings originated from Etruria themselves. With the help of Lars Porsenna, Tarquinius Superbus hoped to regain the kingdom of Rome. Let us read about one man’s valiant efforts to defend the city against this incoming army.

Horatius Defends the Bridge

Use the English paragraphs to prepare yourself for the story which continues into Latin. Once you feel ready, translate the Latin numbered sentences for a grade. Then, answer the grammar questions and short essay questions. Make sure to use the "translation help and new vocabulary."

Lars Porsenna came to Rome with a great Etruscan army. A great terror seized the people of Rome. With everyone frightened by the name of Lars Porsenna, the people abandoned their farms and came into the city of Rome. A bridge made of piles nearly gave way to the enemy, but one brave Roman man, Horatius Cocles, was on the bridge.

Latin Sentences and Questions for Submission

Latin Sentences (translate from Latin to English):

  1. Ubi hostes in ponte erant, Horatius Romanos ad Romam premere iussit.
  2. Horatius viris Romanis dixit, “Ego hostes consistam!”
  3. Horatius a duobus amicis bonis comitatus est.
  4. Hostes ab Horatio et duobus amicis suis retentus est.
  5. Pontus a militibus Romanis delebatur.
  6. Ita Horatius et duo amici sui impetum reliquerunt et ad Romam redierunt.
  7. Horatius, autem, ab Etruscis superatus erat; cum armis suis in Tiberim desiluit.

Horatius swam safely to his Roman allies. Because of his virtue, the Roman people placed his statue in a famous place and gave him a farm.

Grammar and Comprehension Questions (answer in 1-2 sentences):

  1. In Line 2, what is the tense, voice, person, and number, of “consistam?”
    Answer: Present tense, 1st
  2. In Line 3, what is the tense, voice, person, and number of “comitatus est?”
  3. In Line 7, what is the tense, voice, person, and number of “superatus erat?”
  4. Who destroyed the bridge?
  5. How many friends stayed to help Horatius defend the bridge?

Short Essay (answer in 3-4 sentences):

  1. In your opinion, do you believe that Horatius was a heroic figure because of what he did in this paragraph or because he defeated the Etruscans? Provide three to four full sentences explaining your answer, based upon your readings.
  2. Explain how you translated Line 5: Pontus a militibus Romanis delebatur.

    In two or three full sentences, describe the translation steps you took to translate this sentence. In your answer, be sure to include how you found and translated the subject(s) and verb(s), as well as how the cases of your nouns affected their placement in the translation. If this is a passive sentence, don't forget the ablative of agent or ablative of means!

Translation Help and New Vocabulary

1.09 New Grammar to Look Out For!

  • Passive Voice Verbs
    • Tenses: Present, Imperfect, and Future (Present System) and Perfect, Pluperfect, and Future Perfect (Perfect System)

Glossary

  • ubi can mean “where” or “when,” but in this passage, it can only mean one of those. Which one fits best, based on context?
  • facio, facere, fecī, factus,a,um—to do, make
  • cum can mean “against” when speaking of wars/battles against someone.
  • Horatius, Horatii, m. Horatius
  • iubeo, iubere, iussi, iustus,a,um—to order
  • “suis” and “sui” mean “his own.” You’ll learn this pronoun in forthcoming lessons.
  • consisto, consistere, constitī, constiturus,-a,-um—to stop, to stand still
  • comito, comitare, comitavi, comitatus,-a,-um—to accompany
  • paulisper, a little while
  • denique, finally
  • Etruscus, Etrusci, m. Etruscan
  • desilio, desilire, desilui, desultus,a,um—to jump
  • ‘Tiberim’ is the Accusative Case referring to the Tiber River, which acted as a border at one side of ancient Rome. Now, it flows right through the middle of the city.

Nota Bene:

The Perfect Tense, Active Voice uses the 3rd Principal Part (aka the perfect stem). This can look different from the rest of the dictionary entry for that verb. Keep a look out for a few of these in this passage.

Translation Checklist

  • Have you identified the tense, voice, person, number, and mood of the verb?
  • Have you identified the case, number, and gender for the nouns and adjectives?
  • Do your English definitions for each of the words in the Latin translation match the definitions found in the toolbox dictionary?
  • Have you double-checked your Latin vs. English word order?
    • HINT: In Latin, adjectives follow the nouns they modify, genitive nouns follow the nouns they possess, and verbs are typically found at the end of the sentence.
  • Did you transfer all punctuation from Latin to English?
