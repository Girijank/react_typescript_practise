Rome After Tarquinius Superbus

Tarquinius Superbus, the last Roman king, was thrown out of Rome. Afterwards, Rome became a Republic ruled by the leading senatorial class. In the new Roman Republic, two men, Collatinus and Brutus, became the leaders of the new government. However, Roman patrician families of Etruscan descent (the Larcii, Aquilii, Veturii, Tarquitii, and Volumnii) remained in Rome for some time. They tried to assert their aristocratic authority onto the Roman populus.

This led to constant struggles between the conservative class and the populus in Rome. Here’s how:

  • For the next four centuries, Rome and Etruria were at odds. Etruscans began to take over other city-states and neighboring towns called castella.
  • The Etruscans sided with the Greeks and Samnites against the Romans in forthcoming battles. Both the Etruscans and the Romans wanted dominance over the north—the source of major trade routes and points of commerce.
  • The Etruscans continued to be a major influence on Rome’s culture, architecture, politics, and technological advancements.

Culture

  • The move from the single-name system (Romulus) to the double-name system (Julius Caesar) can be attributed to the Etruscans whose double naming system was quite different from the Greeks and others in antiquity.
  • The Roman toga is thought to have been derived from the Etruscan tebenna which was decorated with clavi that indicated a person’s societal rank.
  • The Romans also borrowed the practice of augury from the Etruscans.

Design and Technology:

Known for their craftsmanship in jewelry, design, architecture, and metallurgy, Etruscans shaped architectural style, jewelry design, and battle arms used in Rome.

  • The Etruscan funerary band was a necklace with a heavy ball-shaped pendant that the Romans took and placed on their children’s necks as bullae—good luck charms.
  • The terracotta decoration on the temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus on the Capitoline Hill was designed by the Etruscan sculptor Vulca.
  • The great Etruscan bronze work is best exhibited by the 5th c BCE bronze she-wolf statue, which Rome adopted as the symbol of the Republic.
  • The Roman military utilized the Etruscan bronze weapons as the basis for their military arms, including the basta, the bronze-tipped spear, which can be seen utilized in Etruria from a terracotta slab in 6th c BCE in Caere, and possibly even the Roman gladius.

Domus (Housing):

  • The early Roman oval house, such as the huts on the Palatine, were similarly fashioned from the oval “wattle and daub” thatched roof Etruscan houses.
  • After the middle of the 7th c BCE, oval houses were displaced by rectangular atrium structures, which mimicked the insides of Etruscan tombs and wealthy Etruscan houses like that of the 500 BCE Marzabotto house in Etruria.

In Tuo Orbe!

Etruscans invented the “wattle and daub” house. “Wattle and daub” is the practice of interlacing sticks and twigs with mud and clay for the foundational structure of a house. Still today, we see these types of houses all over the world! Check out Native American houses or medieval European houses to see this same technique!
