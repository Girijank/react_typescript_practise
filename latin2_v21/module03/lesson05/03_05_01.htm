Medical Inquiries

For centuries, medical professionals have asked questions to diagnose and help those who come to them for healing. Ancient Roman doctors did much the same—even though many of them were less than professional!

As an example of a doctor–patient exchange, let's continue the saga of The Doctor and the Soldier's Lost Hand.

"The Doctor and the Soldier's Lost Hand" (Part II)

The dialogue in this scene is between the unfortunate soldier's mater and the medicus the family has come to seek help from. Let's learn a bit about these two:

Name: Mater Marcia

Roman Virtue: Industria

Marcia is a mother who lives in a Roman insulae with her husband and two kids. She teaches her children Latin, of which they can't get enough! They enjoy chanting the interrogative pronouns and adjectives on their daily trip to the market. She wears a long tunic and keeps her hair tidied up when honoring Vesta at her temple in the Forum Romanum. At home, Marcia weaves fabrics at the loom for her family, and she reads the comedic plays of the author Plautus in Latin. With her groma in hand, Marcia is working to become the first female to design the urban grid system of Rome. Marcia does it all!

Name: Medicus Malevir

Roman Virtue: Industria

Medicus Malevir is a doctor who never trained to be a doctor. One might call him a charlatan. He’s a tall, thin man with an arrogance about him that only a non-doctor doctor would have. He hails from Cannae and now lives in a tiny domus right outside the walls of Rome. He walks to Rome daily, though, to drink in the taverns and to find soldiers newly wounded from war who might need his medical “expertise.” His patients pay him lots of money for his questionable services. Thank goodness there’s no medical board in Rome to review his practice!

Quid! Quid dicis? Quis manum eius cepit?

What! What are you saying? Who took his hand

Aliquis manum suam secat; tum canis eum in aqua iecit et in aquam eacum venit.

Someone cut off his hand; then a dog threw it in the water and went into the water with it.

Estne is tutus?

Is he safe?

Ita, canis est nunc siccus.

Yes, the dog is dry now.

Ne! Tutusne filius tuus est?

No! Is your son safe?

O, ita!

Oh, yes!

Quid? De quo cane dicis?

What? About which dog are you speaking?

Non canis meus.

Not my dog.
