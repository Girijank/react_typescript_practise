Question Words

The Romans did not have question marks. Thus, they used question words at the beginning of sentences to propose a question. Remember these question words?

English Question Word Latin Question Word
Who? Quis?
What? Quid?
When? Quando? Ubi?
Why? Cur?
How Quomodo?
Where? Ubi? De quo?
Enclitic–ne

The Enclitic–ne

When -ne is attached to a word, it means that you are now translating a question and not a sentence. The -ne is normally attached to the verb, but you might find it attached to the noun subject as well. Example:

  1. Vir feminam amat. The man loves the woman.
    • Amatne vir feminam? Does the man love the woman?
  2. Aves in caelo volunt. The birds fly in the sky.
    • Voluntne aves in caelo? Do the birds fly in the sky?
  3. Caper a lupa devorabatur. The goat was being devoured by the wolf.
    • Caperne a lupa devorabatur? Was the goat devoured by the wolf?
Nonne

Nonne

Nonne is a Latin word which changes the sentence question to expect a positive answer. The best way to translate the entire sentence question is by adding the word, “surely” and “right” into the translation to help the question expect a positive answer. For example:

  1. Nonne vir feminam amat? Surely, the man loves the woman, right?
    • See how we included “surely” and “right” into the sentence? You now expect a “Yes” answer for the question. That’s how “nonne” works.
  2. Nonne caper a lupa devorabatur. Surely, the goat was being devoured by the wolf, right?
    • See how we included “surely” and “right” into the sentence? You now expect a “Yes” answer for the question. That’s how “nonne” works.
Num

Num

A Latin word which changes the sentence question to expect a negative answer. The best way to translate the entire sentence question is by adding words into the sentence on your own that will help the question expect a negative answer. For example:

  1. Num vir feminam amat? The man doesn’t love the woman, does he?
    • See how we included “doesn’t” and “does he” into the sentence? You now expect a “No” answer for the question. That’s how “num” works.
  2. Num caper a lupa devorabatur? The goat wasn’t being eaten by the wolf, was it?
    • See how we included “wasn’t” and “was it” into the sentence? You now expect a “No” answer for the question. That’s how “num” works.

Vesta’s Advice: Non & Ne

Remember "Non"?

The Latin word "Non" means "not." Therefore, when you see it in the sentence, the translation of the sentence now has a negative word in it. Simply translate the “non” where it lies within the Latin sentence.

Example:

Qui vir feminam "Non duxit? Who did not lead the men into battle?

Remember "ne"?

The Latin word "ne" means "no." Therefore, when you see it in the sentence, simple translate it as a simple "No." .” It is normally an answer to a question. It stands alone in a sentence. Example:

  • Amatne vir feminam ? Ne! Does the man love the woman? No!
  • Caperne a lupa devorabaur? Ne! Was the goat being devoured by the wolf? No!
