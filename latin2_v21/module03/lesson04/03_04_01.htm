The Roman Doctor

Living in Rome and fighting for Rome took its toll on the Roman people. Disease, sickness, and war injuries were commonplace. So-called medical doctors performed strange procedures to help cure the sick and wounded. Let's learn more about these Roman doctors!

Rome’s greatest physician, Galen, who hailed from Greece, discusses his thoughts on the healthcare profession:

"Accordingly, as artisans of health they will all share the same physicians, but insofar as they act with different ends in view, one will be called a lover of mankind, another a lover of honor, another of fame, and still another a money-maker." Galen, On the Doctrines of Hippocrates

A person did not have to be trained to be considered a doctor in Rome! Individuals of all walks of life,be they trained doctors, faith healers, witch-doctors, magicians, and even medical charlatans, could practice what the Romans called the "healing arts." Essentially, anyone claiming to be a doctor could conduct medical procedures.

By the end of the Republic, there had been many accounts both in literature and real life of individuals labeled as a "wicked doctor." These were the doctors who:

  • poisoned their patients
  • murdered their patients at the request of a client
  • mishandled a surgery due to their inadequacies

All of these created a general fear in Rome of the doctor who did more harm than help by using his "medical knowledge." Xenophon, the court-doctor of the Roman Emperor Claudius, was suspected of fatally poisoning the emperor.

The Wounded Aeneas

In Book XII of the Aeneid, Aeneas is wounded by a stray arrow. A doctor, Iapyx, comes to the battlefield to try to heal the wound, but he is not able to remove the arrow. So, Aeneas's mother, Venus, sends down a healing balm to help the doctor remove the arrow from the leg of Aeneas. The doctor then closes Aeneas's wound and Aeneas is ready to fight once more.

Straight from the Source!

The aged Iapyx with his robe rolled back (400)

he shakes the arrow with his right hand and he tugs the sword with tightened pincers. (404)

In Tuo Orbe!

The God Mercury carried the caduceus as one of his symbols. Since Mercury was known as the god of healing, it makes sense that the caduceus is the universal symbol of medicine in the modern world.
