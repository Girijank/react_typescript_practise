Interrogative Adjectives

The interrogative adjective is an adjective paired with a noun to ask a question: “which person?” or “what thing?”

English Formation:

  • The “which?” will refer to a masculine/feminine word.
    Example: Which man walks to the park? Which woman teaches Latin?
  • The “what?” will refer to a neuter word.
    Example: What thing is in the box?

Latin Formation

  • The interrogative adjective is declined in the masculine/feminine and neuter forms.
  • The interrogative adjective matches with its modified noun in case, number, gender.
  • It is important to memorize the interrogative adjective forms. They are not the same as the interrogative pronouns.
  • The plural Latin forms are exactly the same as the relative pronoun plural forms.

Sentence Translations

Interrogative adjectives will be written at the beginning of the sentence. They will be in the case, number, and gender that they need to be, according to the context of the sentence. Here are some examples. Use these as your guide!

Translation Steps:

  1. First, translate the interrogative adjective and the noun next to it.
  2. If the interrogative adjective is not in the Nominative Case, then translate the subject of the sentence next (either a Nominative Case or verb).
  3. Then, translate your verb.
  4. Translate the remainder of the sentence according to correct word order.
  5. Nota Bene: Your interrogative adjective can be in a prepositional phrase. If so, then translate the preposition first, then the interrogative adjective, then its accompanying noun.

Here are examples. Use these as your guide!

Which youth is both bold and swift? Qui iuventus est et audax et celer?
Qui iuventus = Nominative sg. m.
Which woman does not have a suitor on the island of Sicily? Quae feminae in insula Sicilae procum non tenet?
Quae femina = Nominative sg. f.
For what cruel fate does the city of Carthage fall? Cui fato crudeli urbs Carthaginis cadit?
Cui fato crudeli= Dative sg. n.
On account of what omen did Romulus kill Remus? Ob quod omen Romulus Remum necavit?
Ob quod omen = Accusative sg. n.
On which lands have my brave Trojan companions been propelled? In quibus terris mei Troani comites fortes propulsati sunt?
In quibus terris = Ablative pl. f.

Vesta’s Advice: Ablative Preposition + Interrogative Adjective

You will see any of the prepositions (ab/de/ex/cum) which take the Ablative Case written with the Latin interrogative adjective forms.

Example:

  • ab quo / quibus= by which -------?
  • de quo/ quibus = from which ------?
  • ex quo/ quibus = from which --------?
  • quocum/ quibuscum = with which -------?
