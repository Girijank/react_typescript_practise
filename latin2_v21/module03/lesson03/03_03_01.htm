The Urban City

My Vestal Priestesses keep the eternal flame shining bright in my beloved city of Rome. I am honored to have so many people living in a city that never ceases to amaze me.

During the Roman Republic, Rome grew from being a city of small huts to a massive city with a well-defined urban plan that included:

  • paved streets
  • a sewage and drainage system
  • aqueducts carrying water into homes
  • shopping markets
  • assembly halls
  • senate halls
  • banks
  • temples and sanctuaries
  • sports arenas
  • bars and restaurants
  • decorated apartments and townhouses
  • public spaces for gatherings


The Romans even employed city surveyors to map out the construction of the city, using an instrument called a groma to determine the measurements of the grid system. Rome’s urban planning helped pave the way for development of many modern cities today!

Viae : The state funded viae publicae at first for travel for the Roman army. Road construction soon after became part of urban planning for the city. Individuals would be responsible for the building of viae privatae that ran alongside their houses. The streets were lined with large stones at the bottom for drainage, then sand and gravel on top, and followed by another layer of flat paving stones. They built roads over wetlands and mountains, out to their countryside villas, and into other cities. Ultimately, the Romans built over 50,000 miles of roads.

Servian Wall : King Servius Tullius built a wall around the city of Rome to protect it from invaders. Built with stone rocks cut into squares called opus quadratum to stand 71 ft., parts of it still remain standing today.

Cloaca Maxima : An engineering feat constructed under King Tarquinius Priscus, the Cloaca Maxima is a drainage system originally used to clear out the stormwater from the Forum into the Tiber river. The Romans then used the drainage system as a sewage system to help wash sewage from latrines and baths, through the aqueducts, and out of the city. It helped keep the city clean! Today, the Cloaca Maxima is still in use as an auxiliary to Rome’s modern sewage system.

Aqueducts : Beginning in 312 BCE, the Romans began to build aqueducts which were a system of channels, bridges, tunnels, pipes, and canals used to transport water from freshwater sources into the city for drinking water, irrigation, public baths, and fountains. The Romans borrowed the technology from earlier Egyptian and Indian civilizations, and then they created a vast network of aqueducts not only throughout Rome but also throughout their expansive empire—which is why you will find aqueducts still today all over Europe. The famous Trevi Fountain in Rome is still powered by one of Rome’s 1st century BCE aqueducts, the Aqua Virgo.
