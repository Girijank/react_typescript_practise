City Living

"Nobody finds it easy to get to the top if restricted resources cripple his talent. But in Rome, the problem’s worse—inflation swells the rent of your miserable flat, inflation hits the keep on your hungry slaves, your own humble dinner. You’re ashamed to eat off earthenware—yet if transported to a rural peasant regime, you’d be content enough to wear a hooded cloak of coarse broadcloth."—Juvenal, Satire 3, lines 164-170

Roman housing was very different from ours. The first Roman houses were small huts made of wattle and daub. During the Republic, the Romans had more refined housing structures for its growing population. Most Romans, though, lived in the city in large apartment complexes called insulae.

The insulae could be as tall as six stories. They were made from wood with mortar and rubble infill, which made them very prone to fire. On the bottom floor were shops and eateries. The apartments above the shops were tiny and grew smaller and smaller as the building ascended. Wealthier individuals lived on the bottom floors, while the poorer individuals lived on the top floors which were great fire hazards. Nicer insulae would have gardens or fountains or an outdoor courtyard.

Think of the insulae like living/work spaces you might find in a modern city, where there are stores on the bottom floor and then apartments on the floors above the stores. This was Rome! In fact, we might say that many modern city living designs are based upon the living quarters of the city of Rome.
