Iste, Ista, Istud

Remember ille, illa, illud? Well, let us introduce you to this latest demonstrative adjective/pronoun. First, review your Noun/Adjective/Pronoun to remind you of the terms you need for working with pronouns.

English Translation

iste, ista, istud = this/that (singular); these/those(plural). This form differs from hic/ille in this way:

1. There are two ways to translate this form. The translations will depend on the context of the sentence.

  • It is used to describe people who are closer to the speaker: “this (noun) of yours; that (noun) of yours.”
  • Negative connotation: “this (negative meaning); that (negative meaning).” If you want to describe someone or something with a negative meaning, then you will use these forms and insert a negative word such as “nasty or bad” as the negative meaning to describe someone.
    • For example, Scipio Africanus would say of Hannibal: “That bad man is my enemy.” In this example, Scipio would not need to include the adjective “bad.” Rather, he would just use “iste” to describe Hannibal.
Latin Forms: iste, ista, istud

Sentence Examples:

  1. Iste est hostis meus. That nasty man is my enemy.
  2. Istam de Gallia non amo. I do not like that bad woman from Gaul.
  3. Filii isti reginae et regi parere debent. Those sons of yours ought to obey the queen and king.
  4. Ista sunt omnibus nostrum pericula. Those bad things are dangers to all of us.

Ad astra per sententias! Translation practice

Hamilcar Barca dixit, “istos Romanos vincam.”

Hamilcar Barca said, “I will defeat those nasty Romans.”

Dux iste a senatoribus istis necabitur.

That leader of yours will be killed by those bad senators.

Medici isti aegris curas non familiares saepe pro infirmes paraverunt.

Those wicked doctors often prepared unfamiliar cures for the sick.

Milites isti in Gallia pro nobis pugnabunt.

Those soldiers of yours in Gaul will fight for us.
