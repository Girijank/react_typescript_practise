Countryside Living!

You know how important the home is to me, so let’s learn more about the structure of the Roman villa.

During the summer months, wealthy Romans would retreat to their estates in the country to avoid the summer heat in Rome, as well as the noise of the crowds and the smoke from the fires from cooking and heating in the city. These men owned sprawling villas where their family would live and their hired hands would work.

Cato the Elder had a villa in southern Latium that was around 100 acres!

Several Roman writers describe the retreat into the countryside as a means of escaping the negative aspects of living in the city.

Straight from the Source!

The Roman poet Horace writes about how those living in the countryside were free from city influences:

“Beatus ille qui procul negotiis,
utprisca gens mortalium,
paterna rura bubus exercet suis
solutus omni faenore
neque excitatur classico miles truci
neque horretiratum mare
forum quevitat et superba civium
potentiorum limina.”

That blessed man who at a distance from business deals, like the first race of mortals, in the ancestral field his oxen work free from every debt, and neither is a soldier being excited by a trumpet nor does he fear the angry sea, and he avoids the forum and the haughty thresholds of the powerful citizens. (Horace, Epode 2, lines 1–8)

Straight from the Source!

The Roman writer Pliny also describes the virtues of country life and includes how he would enjoy relaxing on his Italian estate:

“Nam super illa quaeret tuli, altius ibi otium et pinguius eoque securius: nulla necessitas togae, nemo accersitor ex proximo, placida omnia et quiescentia, quod ipsum salubritati regionis ut purius caelum, ut aer liquidior accedit. Ibi animo, ibi corpore maxime valeo. Nam studiis animum, venatu corpus exerceo.”

For above those things which I related, their leisure is loftier and more comfortable and more carefree than this: no necessity of a toga, no caller from next door, everything is calm and quiet, which extols itself as a healthy place like the pure sky, like the clear air. There I am healthy in my mind, there in my body especially. For I train my mind in studies, my body in hunting. (Pliny, Letters V, Chapter 6.45–46)
