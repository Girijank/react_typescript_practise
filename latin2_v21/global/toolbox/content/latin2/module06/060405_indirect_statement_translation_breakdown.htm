Indirect Statement Translation Breakdown

How to translate an Indirect Statement:

Step 1: Main Sentence: This has the main “sensory” verb in it. Translate it like any normal Latin sentence.

Step 2: Insert the English word “that”. (Latin will never include a word for “that” in an indirect statement sentence).

Step 3: Now translate the first Accusative you see after the main “sensory” verb. This begins the Accusative–Infinitive clause.

Step 4: Translate the Infinitive (which is normally located at the very end of the entire sentence).

Step 5: Translate the 2nd Accusative (if it exists) you see in the Accusative–Infinitive clause.

Step 6: Translate any other case in the Accusative–Infinitive clause to complete your translation.

You can break any indirect statement down in this way to help your translation:

Subject Main “Sensory” Verb [Accusative-Subject Accusative D.O. {Other Cases/Adverbs} Infinitive]
Nauta audit [monstrum aves ------------- devorare.]
Nauta vidit [monstrum aves celeriter devoravisse.]
----------------- Dicit [virum feminam in Apulia amare.]
Puer scivit patrem eum ad ludum portaturum esse.]

Remember to identify the tense of your main "sensory" verb and your infinitive before translating. The relationship between these two verbs will dictate the tense wording of your English translation.

Indirect Statement Examples

Present Tense Main "Sensory" Verb

Latin English

Sentence

Cives sciunt [provincias ab imperatoribus condi.]

The citizens know that the provinces are being established by the emperors.

Main Independent clause + Sensory Verb

Cives sciunt

sciunt = Present Tense

The citizens know
Indirect Statement clause: provincias ab imperatoribus condidi. the provinces are being established
Accusative-Subject provincias the provinces
Accusative D.O. + Other cases/adverbs ab imperatoribus = Ablative of Agent by the emperors
Infinitive tense, voice, and time condi = Present Passive Infinitive = same time are being established
Latin English

Sentence

Iuventus onstendunt [tela ab artifice Romano facta esse.]

The youth show that the weapons have been made by a Roman craftsman.

Main Independent clause + Sensory Verb:

Iuventus ostendunt

ostendunt = Present Tense

The youth show
Indirect Statement clause: tela ab artifice Romano facta esse the weapons have been made by a Roman craftsman
Accusative-Subject tela the weapons
Accusative D.O. + Other cases/adverbs

No Accusative D.O.

Other Cases: ab artifice Romano = Ablative Absolute

 by the Roman craftsman
Infinitive tense, voice, and time facta esse = Perfect Passive Infinitive = time before have been made
Latin English

Sentence

Poetae cum Maecenate sperat [fabulas suas legatum iri.]

Main Independent clause + Sensory Verb:

Poetae cum Maecenate sperat

sperat = Present Tense

The poets with Maecenas
Indirect Statement clause: fabulas suas legatum iri their stories will be read
Accusative-Subject fabulas suas their stories
Accusative D.O. + Other cases/adverbs
Infinitive tense, voice, and time legatum iri = Future Passive Infinitive = time after will be read
