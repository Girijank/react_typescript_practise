Indirect Statement Tenses—Text Version

Announcer: Every indirect statement in Latin needs three things: a main verb, an accusative subject, and an infinitive verb.

Start your translation by determining the tenses of the two verb forms.

The translation of the infinitive tense depends on the tense of the main verb.

Think of the infinitive forms as points on a timeline, with present infinitives indicating the same time as the main verb, perfect infinitives indicating time before, and future infinitives indicating time after.

For example, "Videt canem ossum capere" is translated as "She sees that the dog is seizing the bone."

Because the main verb, videt, is in the present tense, the present infinitive "capere" is translated at the same time, using "is seizing." [On screen: "Videt" is shown on a timeline in present tense with "capere" above it as the present infinitive. "Capere" is circled in the sentence "Videt canem ossum capere." "Is seizing" is underlined in the sentence "She sees that the dog is seizing the bone."]

If we change the infinitive to "cepisse" in the perfect tense, our translation changes to show time before—"the dog had seized the bone." [On screen: "cepisse" in perfect tense is shown on the timeline before "capere". "Cepisse" is circled in "Videt canem ossum cepisse." and "had seized" is underlined in "She sees that the dog had seized the bone.]

When the verb is changed to the future tense, we see a shift to time after—"the dog will seize the bone." [On screen: "capturum esse" in future tense is shown on a timeline after "capere". "capturum esse" is circled in "Videt canem ossum capturum esse." and "will seize" is underlined in "She sees that the dog will seize the bone."]

But what happens with a past time main verb? "Vidit canem ossum capere." (She saw that the dog seized the bone.)

Since our main verb, vidit, is in the perfect tense, the present infinitive is translated at the same time, using "seized." When the verb is changed to the future tense, we see a shift to time after—"the dog would seize the bone." [On screen: "capturum esse" in future tense is shown on a timeline after "capere".
