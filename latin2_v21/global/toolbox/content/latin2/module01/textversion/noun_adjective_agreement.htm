Noun and Adjective Agreement—Text Version

Announcer: Let's talk about noun-adjective agreement.

An "adjective" is a word that is used to describe a noun.

In Latin, it is important to make sure any adjective you translate describes the correct noun in the sentence. A Latin adjective describes a noun that is the same case, number, and gender. This is called noun-adjective agreement.

Translating with Noun-Adjective Pairs

Let's look at the sentence "Canis puellam bonam amat." This means "The dog loves the good girl."

The girl, puellam, in this sentence is the direct object in the Accusative feminine singular.

[On screen: Text appears "Vir bonus canem amat." "The good man loves the dog."]

Adjectives like "bonus, bona, bonum" follow 1st and 2nd us or a or um endings.

Since not all Latin nouns follow this formula, there are times that the endings on your noun-adjective pair don't match—but they still agree!

In the sentence, "Vir bonus canem amat.", "the good man loves the dog," "vir" (meaning man) is Nominative, singular, and masculine. [On screen: "Vir bonus" is circled in the sentence "Vir bonus canem amat," and "good man" is circled in the sentence "The good man loves the dog."]

Even though it does not match, the adjective "bonus" is the correct form to use with "vir" because it agrees in case, number, and gender.

Just remember, when translating adjectives in a Latin sentence, agreement is everything! [On screen: two hands are drawn on screen, one labeled "noun" and one labeled "adjective." The hands shake as announcer says "agreement is everything."]

Just make sure your noun and adjective are the same case, number and gender.
