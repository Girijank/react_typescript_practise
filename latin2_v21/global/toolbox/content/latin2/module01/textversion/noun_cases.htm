Noun Cases and their Uses—Text Version

Announcer: VO: Nouns are words used to represent people, places, or things.

In Latin, nouns are special because they "decline"—meaning the endings change based on the way they are used in a sentence. [On Screen: Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative, Ablative]

The endings help you know which case your noun is in, since each one has a special purpose.

The Nominative Case is used as the subject—the person or thing that completes the action of the sentence.

The sentence "Agricola feminam amat." means "The farmer loves the woman."

Agricola (the farmer) is in the Nominative case because he is the one who "loves" in this sentence. [On screen: "Agricola" is circled in the sentence "Agricola feminam amat." and "the farmer" is circled in the sentence "The farmer loves the woman."]

The Genitive Case shows possession—indicating ownership using "of" or "apostrophe s"

The sentence "Agricola casam feminae amat." means "The farmer loves the house of the woman."

Feminae (of the woman) is in the Genitive case because it is taking ownership of the house. [On screen: "Feminae" is circled in the sentence "Agricola casam feminae amat." and "of the woman" is circled in the sentence "The farmer loves the house of the woman."]

The Dative Case shows the indirect object—a noun which indirectly receives the action of a verb of giving, showing, or telling.

The sentence "Agricola feminae rosas dat." means "The farmer gives roses to the woman."

Feminae (to the woman) is in the Dative case because she is indirectly receiving the roses, and the verb of this sentence is "dat" (from do, dare, to give). [On screen: "Feminae" is circled in the sentence "Agricola feminae rosas dat." and "to the woman" is circled in the sentence "The farmer gives roses to the woman."]

The Accusative case shows the direct object of a sentence. The direct object directly receives the action of the verb.

The sentence "Agricola feminam amat." means "The farmer loves the woman."

Feminam is in the Accusative case because it receives the action of the verb "loves." [On screen: "Feminam" is circled in the sentence "Agricola feminam amat." and "the woman" is circled in the sentence "The farmer loves the woman."]

The Ablative case has a variety of uses in Latin. Most commonly it is translated as the object of a preposition, following words like "with," "by," or "in." [On Screen: Object of a preposition: the noun translated, following prepositions like "with," "by," or "in".]

The sentence "Agricola in casa habitat." means "The farmer lives in the house."

Casa is the Ablative case because the noun is the object of the preposition "in." [On screen: "Casa" is circled in the sentence "Agricola in casa habitat." and "house" is circled in the sentence "The farmer lives in the house."]

Knowing the use of these most common cases will help you conquer Latin translations! [On screen text appears: Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative, Ablative]
