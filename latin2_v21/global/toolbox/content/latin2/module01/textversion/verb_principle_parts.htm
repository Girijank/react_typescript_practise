Verb Principle Parts—Text Version

Announcer: Ever wonder why verbs in a Latin dictionary have so many parts?

It all comes down to principal parts and how they are used to form Latin verbs!

Verbs are used to describe an action, state, or occurrence. In a Latin dictionary, verbs look like this:

[On screen: Table of verbs] amo, amare, amavi, amatus, -a, -um.

1st PP 2nd PP 3rd PP 4th PP English Meaning
amo amare amavi amatus, -a, -um to love

Most Latin verbs have four principal parts. You'll need to memorize all four principal parts when you learn a new verb. [On screen: "amo," "amare," "amavi" and "amatus, -a, -um" are circled, "to love" has a heart drawn around it.]

Each verb part serves a purpose.

The 1st Principal Part is the Present Tense 1st person singular of the verb.

Amo means "I love."

The 2nd Principal Part is the infinitive of the verb.

Amare, in a sentence, means "to love."

You will also use the 2nd Principal Part to form the stem for all of your present system verbs.

The 3rd Principal Part is the Perfect Tense, 1st person singular of the verb.

For example:

Amavi means, I have loved.

You will also use the 3rd Principal Part for the stem of your Perfect system, Active verbs.

The 4th Principal Part of the verb is the Perfect Passive Participle or "PPP."

amatus, -a, um means, having been loved

Notice that the 4th Principal Part has adjective endings "-us, -a, -um." This is because the PPP will agree with its subject in case, number, and gender. [On screen: "us, -a, -um" are underlined in "amatus, -a, -um"]

The PPP is also used for forming the Passive Voice of the perfect system.

And that's it! Let's put those Principal Parts to work!
