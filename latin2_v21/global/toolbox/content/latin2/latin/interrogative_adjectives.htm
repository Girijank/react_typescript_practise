Interrogative Adjective

The interrogative adjective is an adjective paired with a noun to ask a question: “which person?” or “what thing?”

English Formation:

  • The “which?” will refer to a masculine/feminine word.
    Example: Which man walks to the park? Which woman teaches Latin?
  • The “what?” will refer to a neuter word.
    Example: What thing is in the box?

Example:

  • What book did you see on the shelf?
  • Whose cat is that?

Latin Formation:

  • The interrogative adjective is declined in the masculine/feminine and neuter forms
  • The interrogative adjective matches with its modified noun in case, number, gender.
  • It is important to memorize the interrogative adjective forms. They are not the same as the interrogative pronouns.
  • It does not have an antecedent.
  • It modifies a noun.
  • It must be in a question.
  • The plural Latin forms are exactly the same as the relative pronoun plural forms.

Example Sentences:

  1. Quem equum vir pueris dabit? What horse will the man give to the boys?
  2. Cui Romano Cleopatra cenam bonam preparavit? For which Roman did Cleopatra prepare the dinner?
  3. Quam cenam Cleopatra Marco Antonio preparavit? Which dinner did Cleopatra prepare for Mark Antony?
  4. Ob quod omen Romulus Remum necavit? On account of what omen did Romulus kill Remus?
  5. In quibus terris mei Troani comites fortes propulsati sunt? On which lands have my brave Trojan companions been propelled?
