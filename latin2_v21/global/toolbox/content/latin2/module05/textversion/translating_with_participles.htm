Translating with Participles—Text Version

What is a participle?

Announcer: A participle is a verb that does not act like a verb, because it does not have a subject or conjugate based on person. This is called a verbal adjective.

Like an adjective, a participle declines in order to indicate a noun or pronoun it is describing.

In English you can often spot a participle based on its "-ing" ending.

[On screen: "Does not have a subject," "Does not have a 'person'," "a verbal adjective" and "ing" is circled in "translated using "ing."]

For example, "Puer currens trans lineam advenit." translates as "The running boy arrives across the finish line."

[On screen: "Puer currens trans lineam advenit." Below it, "running" is circled in the sentence "The running boy arrives across the finish line."]

In English, "running" is a present active participle.

In Latin, the "-ns" on "currens" is used to indicate the nominative, singular of this form.

[On screen text: "currens" is circled in the sentence "Puer currens trans lineam advenit."]

Participles agree with their nouns in case, number, and gender.

For example, in the participle phrase "Puer currens" the participle "currens" uses the nominative, singular, masculine "-ns" ending because "puer" is the subject of the sentence.

Now, change the sentence:

"Canis pueri currentis trans lineam advenit." translates as "The dog of the running boy arrives across the finish line." The possessive noun "pueri" is in the genitive singular form. The participle becomes "currentis" to agree. [On screen text: "Of the running boy" is circled in the sentence "The dog of the running boy arrives across the finish line." "Pueri" and "currentis" is circled in the sentence "Canis pueri currentis trans lineam advenit."]

Watch for these participles when you translate, and don't forget the"-ing"!
