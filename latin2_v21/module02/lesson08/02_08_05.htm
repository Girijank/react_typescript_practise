First Punic War (264 BCE – 261 BCE)

What caused the First Punic War? The ruthless Mamertines (followers of Mamers, the Italian god of war) who hailed from Campania, Italy, took by force the Sicilian city of Messana, which was a Greek colony. This made other Sicilian cities mad, including the Syracusan leader Hiero II, who launched a campaign against them. The Mamertines asked both the Romans and Carthaginians for help. They both responded. The Carthaginians already on Sicily sent a garrison to Messana.

  • The Romans sent their consul Appius Claudius Caudex to Messana, but the Carthaginians didn’t allow him to cross the strait of Messana. This angered the Romans who would respond by sending several ships to Messana. The Carthaginians attacked these ships.
  • Hanno, the Carthaginian commander at Messana, decided to release the Roman men he had captured to the Romans. The Romans were insulted by this move. They then gathered 40,000 troops and offered to help the Mamertines declare war, but only against Syracuse and not the Carthaginians.

The Roman Navy

Before the First Punic War, Rome did not possess a strong navy. In fact, it is believed that it probably had at most only 20 ships to its name, but no warships, and thus it had to rely on its allies to supply it with warships. The Romans needed to build more ships! Why?

  • The Carthaginians had the most powerful fleet of 130 warships (and the ability to raise a fleet of 200 warships at any time).
  • The Romans thus seized a Carthaginian ship, brought it on shore, and used the Carthaginian ship as a model to build 100 quinqueremes and 20 triremes of their own in the winter of 261 – 260 BCE.
  • Hand-to-hand fighting on sea: In order to beat the trained Carthaginian seamen at their own game, the Romans either invented or perfected the corvus/corax, a large bridge-like device that could swing by rope onto an enemy ship, latch on with a metal spike, and allow soldiers to cross onto the enemy ship. In this way, a land battle with hand-to-hand combat could be waged on ships. This device helped Rome win the sea battles during the First Punic War.

Sea Battles of the First Punic War

In 262 BCE, the Romans attacked a Carthaginian ally on Sicily which prompted Carthage to defend itself against Rome. This started the First Punic War. The two cities fought a series of sea battles during the war. Click each battle to see who won.

Battle of the Egadi Islands

By the end of these sea battles, the Romans had worn down the Carthaginians. The Romans were able to build up their navy faster than the Carthaginians, employ better battle tactics than the Carthaginians, and had a larger supply of soldiers waiting to fight against a tired Carthaginian army. Throughout, the Romans lost around 1600 total ships, but they had handed Carthage a sound defeat in the First Punic War.

End of the First Punic War

Rome won. Carthage lost. Rome now had control of the Mediterranean Sea. Greek Sicilian cities defected to Rome. Hiero II sued for peace. Syracuse had to hand over all its Roman prisoners of war and pay war reparations to the Romans. Carthage gave up its territory in eastern Sicily to Rome.
