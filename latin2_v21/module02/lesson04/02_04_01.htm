Fabula Fabulosa: Mercury’s Mischief!

“Tum sic Mercurium adloquitur ac talia mandat” (Vergil, Aeneid, Book IV, line 222)

“Thus, at that time he summons Mercury and entrusts such things” (Vergil, Aeneid, Book IV, line 222)

We are enjoying our time learning about the foundations of the Roman Republic and will continue to enjoy it while learning more great grammar. But first… more Mercury mischief! Here I am again with another story about my tricky ways! Let’s listen in.

After sacrificing Apollo’s cattle, I went back to my cave on Mount Cyllene and snuck back into my crib with the hope that my mother, Maia, wouldn’t find out what I did. Well, she did and was not happy with me. She called me a thief and scoundrel and a cheat – oh no, harsh words, Mom! Tum, Apollo ad meum cavum volavit et accusavit me rapiendem bovem eius. But I wittingly told him that I could never have done such mischievous acts as a little baby. I thought he had believed me, but Apollo is a smart god. He called me a magnificent little liar and began to yell at me. Thank goodness, our father, Jupiter, heard the arguments and stepped in to mediate. Our father told us to work in harmony to wrangle the cattle, and we did just that at first—until Apollo found the remains of the two cattle I sacrificed. Oh, he was upset! So, I had to soothe Apollo’s temper with my lyre. He loved the lyre so much that I gave it to him, and now the lyre is one of Apollo’s most beloved possessions. Whew, I barely got out of that one!

In Tuo Orbe!

Pick up a harp or zither stringed instrument and notice how they are very similar to and possibly derivative of the lyre which Mercury invented!
