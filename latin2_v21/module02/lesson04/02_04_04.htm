Translating Relative Clauses in Latin

Translating a relative clause is relatively simple if you follow these steps:

Step 1

Find the relative pronoun in the sentence. Then, find the translation of the relative pronoun in your relative pronoun chart.

  • The relative pronoun will introduce the relative clause.

Step 2

Put parentheses around the relative clause. A rough "rule of thumb": Start with the relative pronoun and go to the next verb. This is the start and end of your relative clause. Translate the relative clause.

Step 3

Underline the antecedent, which will be the word written right before the relative pronoun.

  • The antecedent and the relative pronoun will agree in Gender and Number.
  • Because the relative pronoun is in a different clause, and the relative clause will have its own translation outside of the independent clause, the relative pronoun does not have to have the same case as the antecedent.

Step 4

Translate the entire sentence.

Let’s put it all together!

Example 1
Latin English
Sentence Marcus, [qui meus amicus est], ad portam ambulavit. Marcus, who is my friend, walked to the gate.
Independent clause: Marcus ad portam ambulavit. Marcus walked to the gate
Relative clause: qui meus amicus est who is my friend
Gender and Number of relative pronoun qui = Masculine and Singular because the antecedent Marcus is Masculine and Singular
Case of relative pronoun and explanation qui = Nominative because it is serving as the subject of the relative clause
Example 2
Latin English
Puella, [cuius pecuniam habeo], est Marcia. The girl, whose money I have, is Marcia.
Independent clause: Puella est Marcia. The girl is Marcia.
Relative clause: cuius pecuniam habeo whose money I have
Gender and Number of relative pronoun cuius= Feminine and Singular because the antecedent puella is feminine and singular
Case of relative pronoun and explanation cuius= Genitive because it is serving as the possessor of the relative clause because the money is being possessed Hint on Translating Genitive relative pronouns: Translate the relative pronoun. Next, translate the subject of the relative clause, which will either be a word in the Nominative Case or the verb functioning as the subject
Example 3
Latin English
Liberi, [quibus auxilium donavimus], erant grati. The children, to whom we gave help, were grateful.
Independent clause: Liberi erant grati. The children were grateful.
Relative clause: quibus auxilium donavimus to whom we gave help
Gender and Number of relative pronoun quibus = Masculine and Plural because the antecedent Liberi is Masculine and Plural
Case of relative pronoun and explanation quibus = Dative because it is serving as the Indirect Object of the relative clause because the verb donavimus is a verb that takes the Dative Case Hint on Translating Dative relative pronouns: Translate the relative pronoun. Next, translate the subject of the relative clause, which will either be a word in the Nominative Case or the verb functioning as the subject
Example 4
Latin English
Vir, [quem non amavi], erat Marcus. The man, whom I did not love, was Marcus.
Independent clause: Vir erat Marcus. The man was Marcus.
Relative clause: quem non amavi whom I did not love
Gender and Number of relative pronoun quem = Masculine and Singular because the antecedent vir is Masculine and Singular
Case of relative pronoun and explanation quem = Accusative because it is serving as the direct object of the amavi which is the subject of the relative clause Hint on translating Accusative relative pronouns: Translate the relative pronoun. Next, translate the subject of the relative clause, which will either be a word in the Nominative Case or the verb functioning as the subject.
Example 5
Latin English
Fabula, [de qua scribitur], est fabulosa. The story, about which is being written, is fabulous.
Independent clause: Fabula est fabulosa. The story is fabulous.
Relative clause: de qua scribitur About which is being written
Gender and Number of relative pronoun de qua = Feminine and Singular because the antecedent fabula is Feminine and Singular
Case of relative pronoun and explanation de qua = Ablative because it is serving as a prepositional phrase. The preposition de takes the Ablative case. Hint on translating Prepositional Phrases with relative pronouns: Translate the preposition first. Then, immediately translate the relative pronoun. Next, translate the subject of the relative clause which will either be a word in the Nominative Case or the verb functioning as the subject.

Mercury's Memo: Non

Remember “Non?”

The Latin word, “non” means “not.” Therefore, when you see it in the sentence, the translation of the sentence now has a negative word in it. Simply translate the “non” where it lies within the Latin sentence. Some examples:

Senator, qui feminam non amat, est crudelis. The Senator, who does not love the woman, is cruel.
Equites, qui in proelio non pugnabant, plebes non duxit. The calvary, who were not fighting in the battle, did not lead the plebians.
