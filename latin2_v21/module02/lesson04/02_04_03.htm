Relative Pronouns in Latin

We now know quite well how relative pronouns and relative clauses look and feel in English. So, let’s go one step further and learn how the relative pronouns look in Latin!

Now, let’s learn the meanings of the relative pronouns:

  Masculine and Feminine (singular and plural) Neuter (singular and plural)
Nominative who, which, that which, that, what
Genitive of whom, whose, of which of which, whose
Dative to whom, to which to which
Accusative whom, which, which, that, what
Ablative by/with whom, which by/with, which

Mercury's Memo: Adding "ab, de, ex, cum” to Relative Pronouns

The Latin prepositions “ab, de, ex” will pair with a relative pronoun and will be placed right in front of the relative pronoun in a relative clause. Translate the preposition first, then the relative pronoun. For example:

  • ab quo: away from whom, which; by whom, which (passive verb sentence)
  • de qua: about whom, which
  • ex quibus: from whom, which

The Latin preposition “cum” may attach itself at the end of the Ablative forms to become:

  • quocum: with whom, which
  • quacum: with whom, which
  • quibuscum: with whom, which
