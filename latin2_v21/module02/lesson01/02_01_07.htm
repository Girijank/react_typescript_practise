The Latin Wars

Warfare during this time became a central focus in the civic and religious structuring of many Italian city-states. In Rome, military service was one of the most important duties for a male citizen and because of this, Rome was able to amass a devoted, large army that engaged in warfare against other city-states. Even when Rome would conquer another Italian city-state, the city-state would remain fully independent.

Rome was able to assert itself as the dominant city in Italy during the 6th–4th centuries BCE. During this time, Rome was able to establish its dominance over other Italian cities, in part through a series of wars, called the Latin Wars between 498 BCE – 338 BCE.

The First Latin War

The Latin League : Indigenous group of Italians in the Latium region and Roman colonies who spoke Latin. They fought alongside Rome and against outside tribes who tried to invade after the fall of the Roman Monarchy.

Anti-Roman Alliance: Italian city-states who fought in the Latin Wars against Rome.

In the first Latin War, the Latins were led by the Etruscan leader and expelled king of Rome, Lucius Tarquinius Superbus, and his son-in-law, Mamilius, dictator of Tusculum. The Romans were led by the dictator Postumius and his master of the horse,(magister equitum) Titus Aebutius Helva. After six years of back and forth fighting, the Romans defeated the Latins at the Battle of Lake Regillus around 499 BCE—some even say with the help of the twin gods, Castor and Pollux. But the war did not end until the Treaty of Spurius Cassius (foedus Cassianum) in 493 BCE, which created a mutual military alliance between the Romans and the Latins with Rome as the principle leader between the two.

Not all Latin cities had joined the “Latin League.” Several cities wished to retain their independence and formed alliances with one another against Rome, prompting another Latin War in 341 BCE. Rome was able to defeat the uprising in 338 BCE. From its victory, Rome either:

  • granted citizenship to defeated cities,
  • granted partial citizenship and provided protection to defeated cities, or
  • allowed defeated cities to retain independence but made them give up much of their land for Roman colonization.
