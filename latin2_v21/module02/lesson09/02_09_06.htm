Mercury's Pronoun Tips

Put on your winged cap and sandals because we are about to go on a pronoun ride to the sky! I’ve outlined my tips and tricks to identifying different types of pronouns for our wonderful language. Before you read my helpful tips, review your pronoun charts in the Toolbox. See, I’m a helper too!

The Demonstratives: Idem, Eadem, Idem

The demonstrative idem meaning the same, is a compound of is and -dem, with slight changes for pronunciation. If you have memorized the demonstrative is, ea, id, you already know idem. Just cover up the -dem and see the similarities.

Sentence examples: Try your hand at translating these sentences. Also, identify the case, number, and gender of the demonstrative in each sentence.

Eodem die, dui exercitus ad Cannas iter fecerunt.

On that same day, two armies marched towards Cannae Ablative sg. m.

Avus et filius suus eodem passu circum ripam ambulaverunt.

The grandfather and his own son with the same pace walked around the river bank. Ablative of means: Ablative sg. m.

Iterum fulmen eundem domum percussit.

Again, lightning struck the same home. Accusative sg. m.

Eisdem liberis damus.

We give books to the same children. Dative pl. m.

Eaedem feminae in peristylo villae domini sedent.

These same women sit in the peristyle of the villa. Nominative pl. f.

The Demonstratives:

Hic, Haec, Hoc

Ille, Illa, Illud

In Latin, the demonstrative adjectives are hic (this/these) and ille (that/those). Hic and ille are sometimes translated as the third person pronouns— he (this/that one masculine), she (this/that one feminine), it (this/that one neuter), or they (these/those ones).

Sentence examples: Try your hand at translating these sentences. Also, identify the case, number, and gender of the demonstrative in each sentence.

Ille impetus cum Romanis in Trebia erat magna victoria.

That attack against the Romans at Trebia was a great victory. Nominative sg. m..

Ante meridiem, equitatus illa castra reliquerit.

Before noon, the army will have abandoned those camps. Accusative pl. n.

Is in manibus Graeci medici illius fidem suam ponit.

He is putting his faith in the hands of that Greek doctor. Genitive sg. m.

Hae res et illae curae ponderem equalem non habent.

These matters and those concerns do not hold equal weight. Nominative sg. f. and Nominative pl. f.

Illis fabulam exercituum fortium nostrorum narrabimus.

We will tell the story of our brave army to those men. Dative pl. m.

The Relative Pronouns: Qui, Quae, Quod

When a sentence contains two or more subjects and predicates, the separate parts are called clauses. A relative clause is introduced by a relative pronoun.

Sentence examples: Try your hand at translating these sentences. Also, identify the case, number, and gender of the pronoun in each sentence.

Fluctus, qui sunt magni, naves Troainorum vastaverunt.

The waves, which are large, destroyed the ships of the Trojans. Nominative pl. m..

Maritus coniungem, quae picturas pulchras pingit, amat.

The husband loves the wife, who paints beautiful pictures. Nominative sg. f.

Exercitus a duce, cui multa potestas datur, ducitur.

The army is being led by the leader, to whom much power is given. Dative sg. m.

Etruscani, quorum reges propulsi erant, cum Romanis bella pugnaverant.

The Etruscans, whose kings had been expelled, had fought the wars against the Romans. Genitive pl. m.

Mercurius boves, quos Apollo amavit, necavit.

Mercury killed the cows, which Apollo loved. Accusative pl. m.

Reflexive Pronouns

In Latin, the reflexive pronouns for the first and second persons are identical to the personal pronouns for the 1st and 2nd persons. The only exception is that reflexive pronouns do not have a Nominative case since the reflexive pronoun refers back to the subject of the sentence.

The reflexive pronouns for the 3rd person have their own forms. The singular and plural Latin forms are identical. The gender for the singular form will depend on the gender of the subject of the sentence.

Sentence examples: Try your hand at translating these sentences. Also, identify the case, number, and gender of the pronoun in each sentence.

Pastor se in terram tute iactavit.

The shepherd safely threw himself onto the ground. Accusative sg. n.

Vobis bene steteratis.

You all had stood up for yourselves well. Dative pl. m.

Vestes se non texebant.

The garments were not being woven by themselves. Ablative pl. f.

Naves se in mari crudelibus conficiebant.

The ships were cutting up themselves on the cruel sea. Accusative pl. f.

Meas aures cera claudere mihi persuadeo.

I am convincing myself to close my ears with wax. Dative sg. m.

Reflexive, Possessive Adjectives

Corresponding to meus, tuus, noster, and vester, there is the reflexive adjective suus, a, um (his own, her own, its own, their own). Suus always refers to the subject of the verb. When his, her, etc., do not refer to the subject, then eius, eorum, or earum must be used.

Sentence examples: Try your hand at translating these sentences. Also, identify the case, number, and gender of the reflexive, possessive adjective in each sentence

Cives vestrae imperia sua fregerunt.

Your own citizens broke their own commands. vestra: Nominative pl. f.; sua: Accusative pl. n.

Funus cupiditatum mearum erit pro rostro.

The funeral of my own desires will be in front of the rostrum. Genitive pl. f.

Iudicium civitati nostro tempus dedit.

The court granted a time for our own citizenship. Dative sg. f.

Hostis suus erat Hasdrubal.

His own enemy was Hasdrubal. Nominative sg. m.

Galli ex finibus suis contra Romanos pugnare excesserant.

The Gauls had left from their own borders to fight against the Romans. Ablative pl. m.
