Carthage Nearly Destroys Rome

The Battle of Cannae

In 216 BCE, Rome nearly ceased to exist after the disastrous Battle of Cannae!

The Roman consuls, Varro and Paullus, eager for a win against Hannibal, marched to the city of Cannae in the southeastern Italian region of Apulia where Hannibal had been stationed.

Battle Facts

  • Romans brought 87,000 men; Hannibal brought 50,000 men.
  • Hannibal’s military tactics were too much for the Romans.
  • Hannibal surrounded the Roman troops with Celtic and African infantry that confused the Roman legions.
  • The Romans lost over 70,000 men, and Paullus was killed. About 80 Roman Senators or nobilitates had been killed. Roman military tribunes, quaestors, aediles, and praetors all fell in the battle.
  • Carthage lost only around 6000 men.

Rome Gives Up

Rome lost. Carthage won. The Roman army was utterly destroyed. As a result, Rome had lost hope in itself and debated whether it should sell the Roman nation to a foreign country.

  • Rome’s allies, including Capua, Apulia, and Samnium, defected over to the Carthaginians.
  • The Etruscan towns remained loyal to Rome.

Mourning and Human Sacrifice

  • Roman women took to the streets of Rome to mourn the loss in public.
  • Rome was in utter despair and chaos, without any hope for its future.
  • Rome sacrificed two couples to appease the gods by burying Gallus et Galla and Graecus et Graeca alive in a stone vault.

End of Second Punic War

  • Hannibal continued to raid southern Italy in decisive victories for the Carthaginians.
  • Years later, Scipio regrouped and headed to the city of Carthage itself.
  • Carthage recalled Hannibal to fight Scipio at the Battle of Zama in Carthage.
  • Scipio finally defeated Hannibal, ending the Second Punic War. As a result of this victory, Scipio earned the surname Africanus because he had defeated Hannibal in Africa.
