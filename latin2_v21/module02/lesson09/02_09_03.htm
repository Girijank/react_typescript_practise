Second Punic War (218 BCE – 201 BCE)

After the First Punic War, Carthage had to pay Rome war reparations. It also needed to pay its allies who fought alongside them against Rome. But Carthage was financially exhausted. Therefore, its allies staged a revolt, and Hamilcar had to leave Hispania to quash the rebellion in Africa. Rome took advantage of the Carthaginian unrest to seize the Carthaginian colony of Sardinia. Carthage responded by driving deeper into Hispania under the leadership of Hamilcar.

Once in Hispania, Hamilcar and Hasdrubal amassed a huge army of over 20,000 (including war elephants), gained money from Spanish cities, and took control of the Iberian Peninsula. In 226 BCE, Hasdrubal signed the Treaty of Ebro with Rome to not invade Roman allied territories, but two years later he seized the Spanish city of Saguntum, a Roman ally. Rome was furious and considered this an act of war. The Second Punic War had begun.

Hannibal Marches into Italy

The Romans sent their consul Publius Cornelius Scipio and 60 quinqueremes to Hispania to attack the Carthaginians. They also sent troops to Sicily. But they had miscalculated Hannibal’s brilliant military tactics. Instead of staying in Hispania, Hannibal gathered around 90,000 men, including Celtic allies, northern Africans, and Spanish infantries to march towards Italy through the Alps mountain range. He also left behind troops in Hispania with Hasdrubal and in other cities that he conquered along the way to maintain control over land areas. As he marched, Hannibal’s strategy was to win over Roman allies so that the Romans would have no recourse but to make peace with Carthage on his terms.

Hannibal Crosses the Alps with Elephants

Hannibal crossed the Alps in 16 days, an almost impossible military feat that cemented his legend as a military genius. He crossed with around:

  • 60,000 men
  • 9000 horses
  • 40 elephants in his infantry

The march was so grueling and the winter cold so devastating that by the time Hannibal landed in Italy, over half of his army had perished, only one elephant had survived, and Hannibal himself had gone blind in one eye.

Hannibal in Italy

HIn 218 BCE, Hannibal ambushed the Roman army near the Ticinus River and crushed them. Scipio learned about the defeat and headed back to Rome where he and his troops confronted Hannibal at the Trebia River. Carthage handed Rome another decisively heavy loss. With his great momentum, Hannibal continued to push south towards Rome and handed Rome another huge loss at the Battle of Lake Trasimene in 217 BCE. The Carthaginians killed over 15,000 and captured over 10,000 Romans.

Scipio Africanus then decided to send his brother Calvus to Hispania to intercept Hannibal’s reinforcements being sent by his brother Hasdrubal. However, after three straight battle losses, the Romans replaced Scipio Africanus with Quintus Cunctator Fabius Maximus who employed a battle tactic of avoiding engaging in battle with Hannibal in hopes of slowing Hannibal’s aggression. The Romans grew restless with Fabius’s delaying tactics and replaced him with the consuls, Varro and Paullus.
