Women of the Roman Republic

There are many wonderful women who played significant roles in the shaping of the Roman Republic. Whether they were brave warriors, political leaders, leaders of organizations or religious functions, or women who risked their lives to save the lives of their husbands or their children, there were many revolutionary women of the Roman Republic. We will learn about their stories throughout the course.

Your Latin grammar knowledge will guide you to translate this story about the noble woman, Veturia, who was the mother of Coriolanus.

Veturia's Story

Verbis auctoris Plutarchi, de fabula feminae Romanae rei publicae (of the Republic) discimus. Ei nomen erat Veturia (aut Volumnia), quae erat mater ducis, Gaii Marcii.

By the words of the author Plutarch, we learn about the story of a Roman woman of the Republic. Her name was Veturia (or Volumnia), who was the mother of the general, Gaius Marcius.

Veturia filium, Romanum patricium virum, qui multas terras circum Romam vicit, eduxit. Is Coriolos cepit, et Gaius Marcius Coriolanus appellatus est.

Veturia raised her son, a Roman patrician man, who conquered many lands around Rome. He captured Corioli, and was named Gaius Marcius Coriolanus.

Per haec multa bella, plebes difficiles pugnare recusaverant, quod iura certa cupiverunt.

Through these many wars, the difficult plebeians had refused to fight because they wanted certain rights.

Coriolanus plebibus Romanis illa iura dare on cupivit, et contra eas dixit. Tum, Corialanus ex Roma pulsus est.

Coriolanus did not want to give the Roman plebs these rights, and he spoke against them. Then, Corialanus was pushed out of Rome.

Idem Coriolanus inimicum, Tullum Aufidium Volscorum, excessit et eum et illos eius contra Romam duxit.

This same Coriolanus went out to the enemy, Tullus Aufidius of the Volsci, and led him and his men against Rome.
