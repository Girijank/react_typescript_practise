Relative Clauses

You just learned about English independent and dependent clauses. I am a bit sneaky because I was setting you up to be able to recognize and translate independent and dependent clauses in Latin! First, let’s review our pronoun terms.

Relative Clause

A relative clause is a dependent clause that modifies a word in a sentence.

  • A relative clause contains a relative pronoun + verb and plays the same role as an adjectival clause.
    • A relative pronoun = who, which, that. Remember that a pronoun is a word that stands in place of a noun.
    • A relative pronoun does NOT have to be the subject of the relative clause. There might be another pronoun or noun or verb that is the subject of the relative clause.
    • Word order in a relative clause is DIFFERENT from normal word order in a sentence.
  • A relative clause is preceded by a noun called the antecedent (from the Latin ante + cedere = to go before).
  • The relative clause provides descriptive information about the antecedent.

How it looks in English: The relative clause will generally be surrounded by commas.

The man, who loves the woman, is a sailor.

  • Relative clause: “who loves the woman”
  • Antecedent: “The man”

The king and queen, who have three children, live in the kingdom.

  • Relative clause: “who have three children”
  • Antecedent: “The king and queen”

The sheep, which is standing on the rock, has only one eye.

  • Relative clause: “which is standing on the rock”
  • Antecedent: “The sheep”

The grass, which is green, is being eaten by both the goat and the sheep.

  • Relative clause: “which is green”
  • Antecedent: “The grass”

The king, whose foot is gigantic, is a bad leader.

  • Relative clause: “whose foot is gigantic”
  • Antecedent: “The king”
