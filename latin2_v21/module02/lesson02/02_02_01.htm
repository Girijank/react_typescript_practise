That Republic Lifestyle!

In the mid-Republic, your quality of life depended largely on your social status and class. As the Romans conquered more and more territory, they gradually extended the rights of citizenship to larger groups of people.

As a Roman citizen (civis) you had the right to vote in elections and to use Roman law courts. Only male citizens had the right to wear the toga. Unfortunately, Roman women had no legal rights or status of their own. They were almost entirely dependent on their fathers and husbands.

Slaves (servi) had no rights either. There was, however, a large class of freed slaves in Rome ( liberti) who enjoyed the right to vote but could not run for office themselves. However, the children of freedmen were born free with the full rights of citizenship, which provided a degree of upward mobility that wasn’t available in many other ancient societies.

Social Classes in Rome

Even as a Roman citizen, your life experience varied vastly depending on your social status and wealth. Roman society was largely divided into three social classes:

Senatores—The senatorial class was the ruling class in Rome. These men met the highest requirements for wealth and owned vast quantities of land. They possessed the most power and held the highest political offices and priesthoods, including the consulship. However, the senatorial class made up the smallest percentage of the Roman people.

Equites—The equites (or “knights”) were another wealthy class of citizens whose money came primarily from trade and business (which was seen as inferior to land ownership). These were rich men who lacked senatorial ancestors and a noble pedigree. However, their interests often aligned with those of the senatorial class.

Plebs—The plebeian class was the largest social class in ancient Rome and also the poorest. Although not all plebeian families were poor, most lived very hard lives. They faced inferior living conditions, including fire-prone apartments, and violence on a daily basis. The plebeian class did have its own voting assemblies and could pass legislation. Although it was possible for a plebeian to move up the political ladder, it was not easy or common.

In Tuo Orbe!

These names are still around! In the United States government, we can think of our legislative Senate as its own, very influential, social class. We also have modern day equestrians who ride horses — but now more for recreational purposes. Today, the term plebs carries a negative connotation when applying a “lower class” designation to an individual.
