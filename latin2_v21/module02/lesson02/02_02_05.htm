Rome’s Governing Documents

Although Rome became a Republic in 509 BCE led by two consuls and the Senate, there was a period of time from 451 BCE to 449 BCE during which a legislative group of ten men replaced the offices of the two consuls. This was group was called the decemviri (10 men), short for decemviri legibus scribundis. This was also a period of great unrest between the upper patrician class and the lower plebeian class, due to the inequality of political power and rights among them. At this time, there were no written laws, and the patricians were demanding that laws be written down to protect their rights with regard to their fellow citizens. And so, the decemviri got to work….producing what are known as The Twelve Tables.

Twelve Tables

From historic sources, many of these laws found on the Twelve Tables can be read today. Unfortunately, the Twelve Tables did not survive in totality to the modern era. They were lost in 390 BCE when Rome was sacked by the Gauls. But here is some information from the Twelve Tables that we do have. As you click through this slideshow, remember that these laws were written in the 5th century BCE, so they might seem a bit archaic in our minds. Remember to use your historical lens when reading and interpreting the tables.

Table I: Proceedings Preliminary to Trial

If the defendant attempts evasion or takes flight, the plaintiff shall lay hand on him.

If sickness or age is an impediment, he who summons the defendant to court shall grant him a vehicle. If he does not wish, he shall not spread a carriage with cushions.

Table II: Trial

...a serious sickness... or a day appointed for the hearing of a case with an alien... If any of these circumstances is an impediment for the judex or for the arbiter or for either litigant, on that account the day of trial shall be postponed.

Whoever needs evidence shall go every third day to shout before the doorway.

Table III: Execution of Judgement

Unless the debtor discharges the debt adjudged or unless someone offers surety for him in court, the creditor shall take the debtor with him. He shall bind him either with a thong or with fetters of not less than fifteen pounds in weight, or if he wishes, he shall bind him with fetters of more than this weight.

Table VI: Ownership and Possession
