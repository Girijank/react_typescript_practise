Demonstrative Pronouns/Adjectives: Hic,Haec,Hoc | Ille,Illa,Illud

You have already learned the Demonstrative Pronouns/Adjectives is, ea, id. Let us learn about two sets of Demonstrative Pronoun and Adjectives that are more emphatic. First, be sure to review the Noun/Adjective/Pronoun Guide to learn how we utilize our pronoun and adjective specific terms in Latin.

Set One: hic, haec, hoc (this/these)

Set Two: ille, illa, illud (that/those)

English Construction:

The demonstrative adjectives and pronouns are this, that, these, and those. They are called demonstratives because they "point out" something in a sentence. Before we learn how these forms work in Latin, let’s recall how adjectives and pronouns function in Latin.

Demonstratives may be used as adjectives or as pronouns.

  • Adjective Form : hic, haec, hoc and ille, illa, illud are normally written before the noun it modifies.
    • Singular Translation: hic, haec, hoc as “this (man, woman, thing)”
    • Plural Translation: hi, hae, haec as “these (men, women, things)”
    • Singular Translation: ille, illa, illud as “that (man, woman, thing)”
    • Plural Translation: illi, illae, illa as “those (men, women, things)”
  • Pronoun Form : The demonstratives can stand alone as a pronoun.
    • Singular Translation: hic, haec, hoc as “this”
    • Plural Translation: hi, hae, haec as “these”
    • Singular Translation: ille, illa, illud as “that”
    • Plural Translation: illi, illae, illa as “those”

Sentence Usage:

Adjective Construction :This girl is my friend. (In this sentence, this is an adjective describing “girl.”)

Pronoun Construction :This is my friend. (In this sentence, this is a pronoun because it stands for a noun.)

Latin Construction: First, it is important to see how hic, haec, hoc and ille, illa, illud are declined. Memorize their declining charts.

Demonstrative Pronouns

Hic, Haec, Hoc

Translation: this (singular); these (plural)

Example: This king built a large city. (Hic rex magnam urbem aedificavit.)
