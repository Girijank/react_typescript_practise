The First Pyrrhic Victory

Pyrrhus erat rex fortis Epiri, qui in Romanos pugnavit.

Pyrrhus was the brave king of Epirus, who fought against the Romans.

Pyrrhus ad proelium Heracleae elephantos eius portavit.

Pyrrhus brought his elephants to the battle at Heraclea.

Romani elephantos priusquam non viderant.

The Romans had never seen elephants before.

Viri Romani a pedibus elephantorum fracti et iacti sunt.

Roman soldiers were shattered and flung away by the feet of the elephants.

Pyrrhus and his army fought the Romans at the town of Heraclea. Pyrrhus rode out with three thousand cavalry to meet the Romans, hoping to set the Roman infantry into confusion. However, the Romans had a huge army, and Pyrrhus became startled by the “glimmer of the Roman uniforms.”

Pyrrhus regrouped and decided to bring out his secret weapon—his elephants. The Romans had never encountered elephants prior in any battle. The elephants had towers on their backs filled with armed men from the Epirote army. In fear, the Roman army either fled or were trampled to death by the elephants.

After that, Pyrrhus marched towards Rome and less than 40 miles away from the city, Pyrrhus and the Romans met at the town of Asculum in 279 BCE. Pyrrhus was able to rout the Romans, but he reported that his losses were so great, he could not afford another victory of this sort. Pyrrhus had won the battle, but he lost so many men that he suffered a Pyrrhic Victory.
