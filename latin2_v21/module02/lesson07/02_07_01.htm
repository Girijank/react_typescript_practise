The Pyrrhic Wars

Here comes yet another war for the Romans! Remember, you can see where this war falls in the greater scheme of Roman history on the timeline in your Resources!

Have you ever heard of the phrase, “Pyrrhic victory”? A Pyrrhic victory is when you are victorious at something, but you suffered so much loss that you might wonder if the victory was even worth it.

For example: Say you’ve won a legal case and won $5000 from it. However, you paid over $100,000 in legal fees to receive the victory. This would be called a Pyrrhic victory.

Let's take a look at the original Pyrrhic victory:

Rome had begun to expand its reach into the southern regions of Italy during the fourth century BCE. The southeastern seaboard city of Tarentum, a leading Greek city-state, feared that Rome could threaten its power in Magna Graecia. To stave off Rome’s advancements, Tarentum asked for the help of Pyrrhus, the king of Epirus. Pyrrhus, who wanted to use this opportunity to launch his own siege on the Sicilian city Syracuse, assembled the following to sail to Italy in 281 BCE:

  • 22,500 infantry
  • 2000 archers
  • 500 slingers
  • 3000 calvary
  • 20 Indian elephants

The Romans assembled an army comparable to that of Pyrrhus’s army. Pyrrhus and the Roman leaders tried to negotiate a truce in the opening months of the war, but in 280 BCE, Pyrrhus pitched a camp between the cities of Pandosia and Heraclea which prompted the Pyrrhic War.
