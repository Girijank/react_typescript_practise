Reflexive Pronouns

Now that you are familiar with the personal pronouns and relative pronouns, we will learn the reflexive pronouns. A reflexive pronouns is a pronoun that refers back to the subject of the sentence. Remember that a pronoun is a word that stands in place of a noun.

English examples:

  1. I bathe myself. (The “myself” is referring back to the subject “I.”)
  2. You teach yourself. (The “yourself” is referring back to the subject “You.”)
  3. She wounds herself. (The “herself” is referring back to the subject “She.”)

Before we reflect on reflexive pronouns, review how Latin treats pronouns in your Noun/Adjective/Pronoun Guide.

Latin Construction

In Latin, the reflexive pronouns for the first and second persons are identical to the personal pronouns for 1st and 2nd persons.

  • Exception: Reflexive pronouns do not have a Nominative case since the reflexive pronoun refers back to the subject of the sentence.
The reflexive pronoun for the 1st person
Case Singular Translation Plural Translation
Nominative ----------- -----------
Genitive mei of myself nostrum of ourselves
Dative mihi to/for myself nobis to/for ourselves
Accusative me myself nos ourselves
Ablative me by/with myself nobis by/with ourselves
The reflexive pronoun for the 2nd person
Case Singular Translation Plural Translation
Nominative ----------- ----------
Genitive tui of yourself vestrum of yourselves
Dative tibi to/for yourself vobis to/for yourselves
Accusative te yourself vos yourselves
Ablative te by/with yourself vobis by/with yourselves
The reflexive pronoun for the 3rd person
Case Singular Translation Plural Translation
Nominative ----------- -----------
Genitive sui of himself/herself/itself sui of themselves
Dative sibi to/for himself/herself/itself sibi to/for themselves
Accusative se/ sese (for emphasis) himself/herself/itself se/ sese (for emphasis) themselves
Ablative se/ sese (for emphasis) by/with himself/herself/itself se/ sese (for emphasis) by/with themselves

The reflexive pronouns for the 3rd person have their own forms.

  • The singular and plural Latin forms are identical.
  • The gender for the singular form will depend on the gender of the subject of the sentence.

Mercury's Memo: Adding "with" to Ablatives

The Latin preposition “cum” may attach itself at the end of the Ablative forms to become:

  • mecum: with myself
  • nobiscum: with ourselves
  • tecum: with yourself
  • vobiscum: with yourselves
  • secum: by/with himself/herself/itself
  • secum: by/with themselves
