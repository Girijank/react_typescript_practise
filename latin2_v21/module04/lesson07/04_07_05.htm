Enter Julius Caesar

Now it’s time to meet probably the most famous Roman of them all! I know you’ve been waiting to meet him, so here we go!

Gaius Julius Caesar was born on the generally-accepted date of either July 12 or July 13, 100 BCE, in the city of Rome. His family was a branch of an old patrician clan, or gens, called Iulia (Julia). They are said to have traced their ancestry back to the goddess Venus. Caesar’s branch was called the Julii Caesares. Although Caesar’s family was not wealthy or overly influential, he did have an uncle (Sextus Caesar, 91 BCE) and a distant cousin (Lucius Caesar, 90 BCE) who were consuls.

As his family did not have the money or power to assist Caesar in the very challenging and competitive march up the cursus honorum—the traditional “social ladder,” consisting of progressive public offices beginning with military service and hopefully culminating with the prestigious office of consul or censor, Caesar had to use his intelligence, drive, and leadership skills to assist him.

Caesar’s ability to climb the cursus honorum rather easily made him a perfect example of the Roman ideal of virtus. Virtus was the embodiment of what it meant to be a Roman male—brave, successful in battle, strong of character, intelligent, honorable, and dutiful. As an ambitious man, with constant financial challenges in climbing the cursus honorum, Caesar made a point to make powerful friends, which, of course, also led to powerful enemies.

Although of patrician blood, Caesar was a great supporter of the Populares —a political party supporting greater rights and influence in government for the common people—over that of the Optimates —the traditional nobility. Through his support of the revolutionary Gaius Marius (his uncle by marriage through his aunt, Julia), he gained the ire of the counter-revolutionary leader, Lucius Cornelius Sulla. Sulla also did not appreciate that fact that Caesar refused to divorce his own wife, Cornelia, to marry Sulla’s daughter.

After returning from military service in 78 BCE, Julius Caesar began his march up the cursus honorum, being elected military tribune in 72 BCE, quaestor in 69 BCE, curale aedile in 65 BCE, pontifex maximus in 63 BCE, praetor in 62 BCE, governor of Further Spain in 61 BCE, and consul in 59 BCE.
