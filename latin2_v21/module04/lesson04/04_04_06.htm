"Regulus and the Giant Snake!"

One of the greatest heroic inspirational stories ever told by the Romans was the story of the Roman soldier, Atilius Regulus. This famous soldier defeated a giant Carthaginian snake in the Punic Wars. The Legend

The city of Carthage had giant snakes that the Romans had never seen before. When the Romans, under the leadership of Atilius Regulus, sailed to Carthage in the First Punic War, his men came upon a snake of enormous size near their camp at the Bagrada River. Legend says that the snake was 120 feet in length!

The men were startled at the sight and attacked the snake by throwing their javelins and darts at it. But their weapons slid off the snake’s slippery reptilian hide. The snake proceeded to dart into the Roman camp, killing many of the soldiers by sheer force or with its poisonous breath. It swallowed men whole. It uprooted trees from their roots. It slashed its coiled body onto the men. Regulus saw the fear in his men’s eyes and quickly took action, gathered up his cavalry, and called out to his men:

serpentine Itala pubes terga damus Libycisque parem non esse fatemur angibus Ausoniam? —Silius Italicus, Punica, book VI, lines 242–244

Then, what happened?

Acrius instant, iamque alvo penitus demersa falarica sedit,et geminum volucres lumen rapuere sagittae. —Silius Italicus, Punica, book VI, lines 272–274

…and does Regulus finally kill the giant snake?

Regulus spurred forth on his horse to personally strike the head of the snake with his spear. His men marveled at his bravery and were inspired. They began to throw more arms at the snake, and Regulus set forth his ballista to fire stones at it. The stones were launched at the snake’s belly, and the army’s incessant arrows caused the snake to drop, numb and wounded, on the ground. The snake had fallen!

The Story’s Purpose: Remember, Rome didn’t like Carthage. How does this story depict Roman heroism over Carthage? The slithering snake imagery also allowed Rome to perpetuate a negative image of the African nation
