Authentic Idioms

Roman authors insert Latin idioms into their writings all the time! Let’s see some of them in their authentic forms.

Latin Idiom Idiom Translation Idiom in Authentic Latin Sentence Latin Sentence Translation
se emergere ex malis to recover from misfortune Quibus ex malis ut se emersit, nihil aliud egit quam ut quam plurimis, quibus rebus posset, esset auxilio. (Nepos, Atticus, 11.1) As he emerged from which misfortunes, he had nothing else other than he was an aid to as many people as possible, by whatever matters he could.
imber tenet the rain continues imber continens per noctem totam usque ad horam tertiam diei insequentis tenuit. (Livy, ab Urbe Condita, 23.44.6) The rain continuing through the entire night and holds all the way up to the third hour of the following day.
rem/ ceteram paucis absolvere to explain a matter briefly Cetera quam paucissimis absolvam. (Sallust, Iugurtha, 17.2) I will explain the rest with as few words as possible.
vox magna, clara a strong, loud voice …de carcere magna et queribunda voce dicebat. Cicero, pro Sulla, 10.30) …he spoke with a loud querulous voice about the prison.
molestiam alicui aspergere to be pedantic Nunc mihi iucunditatis plena epistula hoc aspersit molestiae, quod videris, ne mihi molestus esses, veritus esse atque etiam nunc vereri (Cicero, Letters to his brother Quintus, vol. 2) Now comes a delightful letter, but with this drop of gall in it—that you seem to have been afraid, and still to be afraid, that you might bore me.
