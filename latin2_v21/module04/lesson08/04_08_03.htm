Translating with Idioms

Let’s use the idioms in your vocabulary and apply them to sentences!

Steps to understand idiom translation:

  1. The idiom must fit into the sentence with correct grammatical structure. Therefore:
  2. Identify the words in the sentence that create the idiom.
  3. Then, the verb of the idiom can be conjugated into any tense, voice, person, or number.
  4. The noun/adjective of the idiom can be declined in any case.
  5. Translate the idiom, according to its grammatical structure, while adhering to how the idiom translates into English.

Let’s see this in practice!

Latin Sentence English Translation Idiom and Meaning Grammar Explanation  
Proelium inter Pompeium et Caesarem in Thessalia commissum erat . A battle between Pompey and Caesar had been begun in Thessaly. Proelium commissum erat = had been begun Proelium is the Nominative Case because it is the subject of the sentence.
The verb commissum erat is the pluperfect tense, Passive voice, 3rd person singular to match with the 3rd person singular noun subject, Proelium.
Caesar Cleopatram in Aegypto adiuvare consilium cepit. Caesar adopted a plan to help Cleopatra in Egypt. consilium cepit = adopted a plan Consilium is the Accusative Case because it is the direct object of the verb.
The verb cepit is the perfect tense, Active voice, 3rd person singular to match with the 3rd person singular noun subject, Caesar.
Caesar bellum contra Senatum geret. Caesar will wage war against the Senate. bellum geret = will wage war Bellum is the Accusative Case because it is the direct object receiving the action of geret.
The verb geret is the future tense, Active voice, 3rd person singular to match with the 3rd person singular noun subject,Caesar.

Hints for Developing "Word Senses" with Idioms

Few words in any language have exactly the same meaning at all times.

  • While words usually have one general meaning, they may have several shades of meaning, which depend entirely upon their context, or surroundings.
  • In translating a Latin word, therefore, it is necessary to get its exact meaning (as opposed to its general or "vocabulary" meaning) from its context or setting.

Let’s take a look at these idioms below. You’ll see that they all contain the common word “ago, agere, egi, actus,-a,-um = to do, drive.” But, when this verb is placed inside of an idiom, it will have multiple meanings.

Your goal: Memorize the chart below, and see how these idioms work in action!

Idioms using ago, agere

Latoya multum in officiis legis agit. Latoya does much in the duties of the law. multum agit = does much The verb agit is the present tense, Active Voice, 3rd person singular to match with the 3rd person singular noun subject, Latoya .Multum is the Accusative Case because it is the direct object of the verb.
Milites Caesaris praedam belli Gallici agent. The soldiers of Caesar will carry off the loot of the Gallic War. praedam agent = will carry off the loot The verb agent is the Future tense, Active Voice, 3rd person plural to match with the 3rd person plural noun subject, Milites. Praedam is the Accusative Case because it is the direct object of the verb.
