The Second Civil War: Veni, Vidi, Vici!

Caesar crossed the Rubicon is probably one of the most well-known phrases from classical studies. Let’s learn about why he crossed the Rubicon River and how the crossing helped Caesar become Rome’s most powerful, but feared leader.

Alea Iacta Est!

Caesar had successfully fought a series of wars against the Gauls between 58 BCE and 52 BCE. He wrote a first-hand account of these wars in his work titled, “De bello Gallico.” After securing most of Gaul for Rome in 50 BCE, Caesar was expecting a Triumph from the Roman Senate. However, the Senate now feared Caesar’s ever-growing popularity and power and thus denied him a Triumph.They also denied his candidature request for consul. Caesar’s ego had been bruised, so he gathered 40,000 of his soldiers, left his post in Gaul, and crossed the Rubicon River in Italy, which garnered a similar meaning to when Sulla Marched on Rome. In 49 BCE, by Crossing the Rubicon, Caesar had declared war on Rome, but most specifically, on the Senate. This was the beginning of the Second Civil War.

Caesar versus Pompey

Pompey, who controlled the Senate in which there were a vast majority of men hostile towards Caesar, waged battles against Caesar on behalf of the Republic. Pompey was a statesman and part of the old Republican guard which sought to maintain a level of dignitas within the Senate. He and the Senate thought Caesar’s Crossing of the Rubicon was an insult on this dignitas. Here’s how their bouts unfolded:

  • Pompey first headed to Greece to gather forces. Knowing that Pompey had allied support in Spain, Caesar went to Spain to neutralize Pompey’s troops there.
  • Then, Pompey and Caesar and their armies faced off in Greece. Pompey had a far superior army and believed he could win against Caesar.
  • In 48 BCE, their two armies fought on the Grecian coastal city of Thessaly. Caesar’s veteran army won, and Pompey escaped to Egypt where he was murdered by its king.
  • Caesar, not knowing of Pompey’s death, sailed to Egypt where he was supported by the Egyptian queen, Cleopatra. He then returned the favor to Cleopatra by fighting wars in Egypt for her to help her secure her throne.

Veni, Vidi, Vici!

On his way home from Egypt,Caesar said these famous words after waging a minor battle against King Pharnacesin Pontus. On his way back to Rome from Egypt, Julius Caesar fought small battles along the way, including defeating King Pharnaces in the city of Pontus, which you might remember was once ruled by King Mithridates VI.

Caesar definitely came, saw, and conquered! He conquered the Gauls, Rome, Pompey, and Anatolia and returned home to Rome in 46 BCE, victorious. He was then given the title of Dictator and squashed any opposition to his power. He killed Pompey’s son, defeated Spain and made it a province, and finally celebrated that Triumph he had always wanted in 45 BCE. The Second Civil War was now over.
