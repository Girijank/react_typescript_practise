Degrees of Adjectives

Adjectives change form to show degree of comparison. There are three degrees: positive, comparative, and superlative.

The positive is the simple form of the adjective. This is the degree you know so far. For example:

  • the brave man
  • the tall woman
  • the good thing

The comparative is used when you compare one noun to another. For example:

  • the braver man
  • the taller woman
  • the better thing
  • can be translated as follows:
    • -er: happier, quicker, slower
    • more (adjective): more happy, more quick, more slow
    • too (adjective): too happy, too quick, too slow

The superlative is used when you have three or more nouns to compare. For example:

  • the bravest man (assumingly out of three or more people)
  • the tallest woman (assumingly out of three or more people)
  • the best thing (assumingly out of three or more people)
  • can be translated as follows:
    • -est: happiest, quickest, slowest
    • most (adjective): most happy, most quick, most slow
    • very (adjective): very happy, very quick, very slow

Remember these common adjectives? Watch out for them in your translations!

  • Positive: Earl is tall.
  • Comparative: Byron is taller.
  • Superlative: Luther is the tallest of them all.
  • Positive: Burt is handsome.
  • Comparative: Chauncey is more handsome than Burt.
  • Superlative: Ollie is the most handsome of the three men.
