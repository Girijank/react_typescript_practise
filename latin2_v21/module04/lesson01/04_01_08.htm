Comparative Adjectives Sentence Usage

Let’s learn how to translate an adjective of the comparative degree in a Latin sentence. You will learn that there are three ways of identification.

Simple Comparative Adjective

It can simply modify a noun in a sentence. Remember that all adjectives, no matter their degree, will always modify a noun in case, number, and gender.

Latin Sentence English Translation Comparative Identification + Explanation
Deus fortius in proelio pugnat. The braver god is fighting in the battle. fortius = Nominative sg. m. to agree with deus.
Vir cum fratre audaciore in proelio pugnat. The man with a braver brother is fighting in the battle. audaciore = Ablative sg. m. to agree with fratre.
Quam + Comparative

It compares one person to another person in the sentence. This type of sentence needs the word “quam = than” to complete the sentence construction. The two people being compared must be placed in the exact same case to show true comparison.

Latin Sentence English Translation Comparative Identification + Explanation
Virum altiorem quam illum non vidi. I have not seen a man taller than that man. altiorem = Accusative sg. m. to agree with virum. Illum is Accusative because virum is Accusative. Those are the two people being compared.
Deus fortior quam miles erat. The god was stronger than the soldier. fortior = Nominative sg. m. to agree with deus. Miles is Nominative because deus is Nominative. Those are the two people being compared.
Ablative of Comparison

It compares one person to another person in a sentence, but the second person in the comparison will be in the Ablative case without a preposition. You will translate this Ablative of Comparison by inserting the word “than” in English before the compared noun translation.

Latin Sentence English Translation Comparative Identification + Explanation
Puellam pulchriorem ea non vidi. I have not seen a prettier girl than she. pulchriorem = Accusative sg. f. to agree with puellam. ea is in the Ablative case because it is the second word in the comparison. It is in the Ablative of Comparison.
Deus fortior milite The god was stronger than the soldier. fortior = Nominative sg. m. to agree with deus. milite is in the Ablative case because it is the second word in the comparison. It is in the Ablative of Comparison.

Ad astra per sententias!

Equitatus diviores quam infantres est.

The cavalry is richer than the infantry.

Miles Romanus fortior milite hostium est.

The Roman soldier is braver than the soldier of the enemy.

Asinos utiliores in agris vidimus.

We saw the rather useful donkeys in the fields.

Aes gravius quam argentum in stipendio nostro dederant.

They had given copper heavier than the silver in our stipendium.

Ea condicio inter Romanos et Graecos omnibus populis facilius erat.

This agreement between the Romans and Greeks was too easy for all the people.
