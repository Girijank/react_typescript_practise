Units and Weaponry

The Roman army was very tightly structured and contained officer rankings to which the Roman soldiers would aspire.

Officers

  • Generals and the legatus: A consul or person of Praetorian rank could command an army. The legatus was in charge of the legion. He could issue punishments, wield absolute power, and gain large sums of war booty.
  • Senior officers: Seven officers, normally of senatorial or equestrian rank, were the senior officers. The highest position a normal citizen could reach was the praefectus castrorum, or camp-prefect.
  • Field officers: They were otherwise called centurions . There were sixty centurions, each of whom commanded 80 men. They trained and disciplined the soldiers.
  • Non-Commissioned officers: The non-commissioned officers worked on special projects for the army such as carpentry, stone masonry, and musical accompaniment. They could rise up in rankings and receive higher pay if they showed ambition and promise.

Infantry

The Infantry was the strongest fighting unit of the Roman army, which consisted of the velites, hastati, and triari.

  • Velites: The light-armed infantry who fought in hand-to-hand combat with short swords
  • Hastati: The men in their early twenties or late teens who fought on the front line with a sword and spear
  • Principes: The heavy infantry who fought in the second line behind the velites and were equipped with a sword and better armor than the hastati
  • Triarii: A heavy infantry in their thirties or forties who were the last line of the infantry and were the best equipped and armored men

The cavalry

  • Was the lightly-armored horse-riding men of the Roman army who were the richest men in the army
  • Served as scouts on the battlefield
  • Was positioned on the flanks of the army
  • Chased down fleeing enemies on their own horses

Weapons

Soldiers were equipped with a variety of weapons:

  • Gladius: Short sword used for hand-to-hand combat
  • Pilum: Javelin carried and launched by the frontline infantry
  • Purgio: Dagger of 20cm. in length, carried by all soldiers

Mismatched Uniforms

Can you imagine showing up to battle in your everyday clothes? Well, that’s exactly what was happening in Rome’s military. Early on, there was no standard uniform for the army, so men wore their native garb. Men fought in their native armor and also, most likely, in their native fighting style. Then, Rome outfitted wealthier men with more armor than their poorer companions. As the Romans solidified their army, their standardized uniform was a wool tunic or cloak, a helmet, shield, padded body armor, shoes, and greaves.
