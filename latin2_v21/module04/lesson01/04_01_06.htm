Comparative Adjectives Latin Formation

Let’s now learn how to form the comparative degree of an adjective in Latin. But, before we do, let's review how an adjective works in a Latin sentence.

In Latin, the comparative is formed using this formula:

base of adjective- ending + -ior (m. and f.); -ius(n.)

Comparison of Adjectives
Remember the stem/base of the positive degree adjective is found in its Nominative singular feminine.
Positive Translation Comparative Translation
altus (m), -a (f), -um (n) high altior, altius higher
fortis (m & f), forte (n) brave fortior, fortius braver

Comparative adjectives always use 3rd Declension endings. Declining steps:

Comparative of 1st/2nd Degree Adjectives

Declining steps: altus, alta, altum-high, deep, tall

  1. Go to alta (Nominative singular feminine form).
  2. Chop off the ending -a.
  3. Add the -ior (m./f.); -ius (n.) for Nominative singular.
  4. For declining of the remaining cases, add the -ior, and then add 3rd Declension endings.
  5. English Translation: higher, rather high, too high
  Singular   Plural  
m/f n m/f n
Nominative altior altius altiores altiora
Genitive altioris altioris altiorum altiorum
Dative altiori altiori altioribus altioribus
Accusative altiorem altius altiores altiora
Ablative altiori altiori altioribus altioribus
Comparative of 3rd Declension Adjectives

Declining steps: fortis, forte-brave, strong

  1. Go to fortis (Nominative singular feminine form).
  2. Chop off the ending -is.
  3. Add the -ior (m./f.); -ius (n.) for Nominative singular.
  4. For declining of the remaining cases, add the -ior, and then add 3rd Declension endings.
  5. English Translation: braver, rather brave, too brave
