Adjectives: Superlative Degree

You now know both the positive degree and the comparative degree of adjectives. Now, let’s learn the final degree—the superlative!

The superlative is used when you have three or more nouns to compare. For example:

  • the bravest man (assumingly out of three or more people)
  • the tallest woman (assumingly out of three or more people)
  • the best thing (assumingly out of three or more things)
  • can be translated as follows:
    • -est: happiest, quickest, slowest
    • most (adjective):most happy, most quick, most slow
    • very (adjective):very happy, very quick, very slow

We will first learn how to form the superlative in Latin for positive degree adjectives which end in “-us,-a,-um” and 3rd Declension adjectives ending in “-is, -e.” The superlative is formed using this formula:

base of adjective - ending + -issimus,-a,-um

Remember, the stem/base of the positive degree adjective is found in its Nominative singular feminine.

Now let’s learn the Superlative Degree in Latin!

Positive Translation Comparative Translation Superlative Translation
altus (m), -a (f), -um (n) high altior, altius higher altissimus, a, um highest
fortis (m & f), forte (n) brave fortior, fortius braver fortissimus, a, um bravest
Superlative of 1st/2nd Declension Adjectives

Decline the superlative degree in the 1st and 2nd Declension only.

Declining Steps: altus, alta, altum

  1. Go to alta.
  2. Chop off the ending -a.
  3. Add the -issimus,-a,-um endings according to the correct gender.
  4. The English translation is: tallest/ most tall/ very tall.

Superlative of 3rd Declension Adjectives

Decline the superlative degree in the 1st and 2nd Declension only.

Declining Steps: fortis, forte

  1. Go to fortis (Nominative sg. f.)
  2. Chop off the ending -is.
  3. Add the -issimus,-a,-um endings according to the correct gender.
  4. The English translation is: strongest/most strong/very strong.
