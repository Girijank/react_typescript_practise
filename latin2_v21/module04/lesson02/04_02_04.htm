Great Rebels!

Let's learn about those rebel rousers who dared to go against Rome!

Jugurtha (164 BCE-102 BCE)

Jugurtha was a fearless leader. At first, he was beloved by the Romans, respected by the Numidians, and then later feared by his family, countrymen, and the Roman state.

Facts:

  • He was the grandson of Massinissa, who had helped Scipio Africanus in the Second Punic War.
  • His uncle, Micipsa, who had become the Numidian leader, had left one-third of the Numidian kingdom to Jugurtha and the other two-thirds to Jugurtha’s cousins.
  • He wanted the entire Numidian kingdom for himself, so he killed his cousin Hiempsal, which pushed Numidia into a civil war.
  • He pursued his other cousin Adherbal, who had asked Rome for help against Jugurtha. The Romans sent Sulla, Marius, the consul Metellus, and Italian elite soldiers to rout out Jugurtha.
  • Jugurtha killed Adherbal and Roman soldiers in the town of Cirta.
  • He was betrayed by his son-in-law, Bocchus, who turned him over to Rome where he was executed.

Social Wars (91 BCE-87BCE)

Italian allies had fought on behalf of Rome and alongside Rome for centuries now, and always with the promise of being granted Roman citizenship.

Facts:

  • The allies had had enough! Rome had no intention of granting them citizenship.
  • The allies rose up in rebellion against Rome in 91 BCE. Caught by surprise, Rome lost the early battles and therefore granted citizenship to the allies.
  • Rome struck back with all its might! With seasoned leaders such as Marius and Sulla, Rome subdued the rebellion, and the allies folded in 87 BCE.

King Mithridates VI (135 BCE- 63 BCE)

Mithridates was the son of King Mithridates V of the city of Pontus in Turkey.

Facts:

  • He wanted absolute power, so after his father’s death, he assassinated his mother and brother.
  • He went on a conquering spree all over the northern coast of the Black Sea, Anatolia, and into Italian territory to help the allies in the Social War.
  • He then set out to assassinate all Romans and Italians living in the province of Asia. Rome struck back and went into a series of three wars against Mithridates from 87 BCE–63 BCE.

Cogitemus! Fact or Myth?

Have you heard of the term Mithridatic poisoning? Mithridates VI was so fearful he would be poisoned by someone that he would ingest small doses of poison (in a chewing tablet form) on a regular basis, with the hopes of creating immunity from poison. Supposedly, the concoction contained small amounts of arsenic and venom.
