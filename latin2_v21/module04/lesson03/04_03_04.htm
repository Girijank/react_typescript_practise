Fabula Fabulosa: I’m a Lover and a Fighter

I love great women, too! Hear how I became known for not only being a great fighter, but also a very romantic god. Allow me to tell you all about the women I admired.

Venus

Sum amator et pugnator. I fell in love with the beautiful goddess Venus, who was already married to my brother Vulcan. I thought Vulcan would never find out about our secret embraces, but one day he devised a trap, an invisible net—literally and figuratively—to catch me and Venus together. Then, to humiliate us further, he called down all of my fellow gods from Mt. Olympus to see us caught in his net. Yet, it was embarrassing, but honestly, I think more so for him than for me!

Minerva

I moved on from Venus and fell in love with the incredibly wise goddess, Minerva, but unlike Venus, she rejected me. But, I’m a fighter (and I guess a lover too). So, I fought for her love by seeking the help of the Carthaginian queen Dido’s sister, Anna Perenna, who was turned into a river nymph after moving to Aeneas’ new home of Latium in Italy. I thought she could help me persuade Minerva to fall in love with me; instead, Anna herself fell in love me and disguised herself as Minerva with a veil and tricked me into marrying her! After that, our union was forever celebrated on the Ides of March with a festival by young girls. Mars et Anna in perpetuum.

Nota bene:

You can read more about my romances in Homer’s Odyssey, 8.276–281 and Ovid’s, Fasti III.
