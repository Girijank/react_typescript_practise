Comparative and Superlative of Adverbs

Remember adverbs? The adverbs you know are in the positive degree, and they looked like this:

An adverb modifies or describes a verb, an adjective, or another adverb.

Formation of Adverbs from the Positive Degree Adjectives

  • First and Second Declension adjectives add “-e” to the base.
    Example: late (widely), pulchre (beautifully), clare (clearly)
  • Third Declension adjectives add “-iter” or “-ter” to the base.
    Example: breviter (briefly), pariter (equally), audaciter (bravely)

Now we will learn the comparative and superlative degrees of adverbs and their sentence usage. Select the tabs to understand how it works!

Comparative Adverbs

The comparative of adverbs is simply the Nominative neuter singular form of the comparative adjective.

Positive Adverb Comparative Adverb
alte - highly altius - more highly
fortiter - bravely fortius - more bravely

Comparative Adverb Examples:

  1. celerius: more swiftly
  2. audacius: more boldly
  3. facilius: more easily
  4. crudelius: rather cruelly
  5. sacrius: more sacredly
Superlative Adverbs

The superlative of adverbs uses this formula:

base of superlative adjective - ending + -e

Adverb forms for –er,-lis Adjectives

The positive adverbs formed from these adjectives are generally formed regularly, but the adverb for facilis is facile, not faciliter. In the superlative, the corresponding adverbs end in -e: facillime.

Positive Adverb Comparative Adverb Superlative Adverb
alte - highly altius - more highly altissime - most highly
fortiter - bravely fortius - more bravely fortissime - most bravely
facile – easily facilius – rather easily facillime– most easily
celeriter – quickly celerius – rather quickly celerrime – most quickly
dissimiliter – dissimilarly dissimilius – rather dissimilarly dissimillime – most dissimilarly

Superlative of Adverbs Examples:

  1. latissime: most widely
  2. gravissime: most seriously
  3. ignotissime: most unknowingly
  4. clarissime: most famously

Mars' Missive: Identifying Comparative and Superlative Adverbs in Sentences!

Comparative adverb

  • If you see a Nominative neuter singular comparative form, you will use the sentence context and the word order to determine if the word is a comparative adjective or comparative adverb.
  • If the word is placed right before the verb, it is most likely a comparative adverb.
  • English translation: Translate with “more” or “rather,” and then add “-ly” to complete the translation.

Comparative Examples

Latin Sentence English Translation
Mercurius lyram pulchrius lusit. Mercury played the lyre rather beautifully.
Jugurtha in bellis eius audacius pugnavit. Jugurtha fought rather boldly in his wars.
Mithradates provincias Romanas Asiae et Africae crudelius oppugnavit. Mithradates attacked rather cruelly the Roman provinces of Asia and Africa.

Superlative adverb

  • The superlative adverb most likely will be placed right before the verb in the sentence.
  • English translation: Translate with “very” or “most,” and then add “-ly” to complete the translation.

Superlative Examples

Latin Sentence English Translation
Hic se gravissime vulneravit. He wounded himself most seriously.
Mars in Troiano bello fortissime non pugnavit. Mars did not fight most bravely in the Trojan War.
Spartacus de fortibus eius clarissime cognotus est. Spartacus was known most famously for his bravery.
