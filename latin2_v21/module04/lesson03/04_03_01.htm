Rise Up, Spartacus!

You might have heard of him — Spartacus! He has been represented in many different forms of modern media, from novel to movie to television drama. According to legend, Spartacus was:

  • a Roman allied soldier from Thrace, who escaped his legion, was captured by the Romans, and then enslaved as his punishment
  • sent to Capua to train as a gladiator where in 73 BCE, he and Crixus, a Gaul, would organize a group of gladiatorial slaves and escape their training grounds

The Rebellious Crew

Spartacus and his followers traveled to Vesuvius, picking up fellow supporters of other runaway slaves, farmers, or free workers along the way. Their goal libertas!

  • Some 30,000–70,000 men had now joined the rebellion and were able to defeat the Romans at major battles.
  • Crixus and Spartacus separated their legions and headed in different directions.
  • Crixus and his men were defeated in 72 BCE.

Straight from the Source!

Nec abnuit ille de stipendiario Thrace miles, de milite desertor, inde latro, deinde in honorem virium gladiator. (Florus, Epitome Rerum Romanorum, 2.8.20.8-9.)

Nor did that soldier refuse to fight, as a Thracian mercenary, as a military deserter, then as a brigand and finally, in honor of his strength, a gladiator. (Florus, Epitome Rerum Romanorum, 2.8.20.8-9.)

Romans Strike Back

The Romans sent Crassus to pursue Spartacus and was able to blockade Spartacus’s army near Rhegium.

  • Spartacus, in vain, tried to negotiate their release with the Roman leader, Pompey. Spartacus and his army, however, were able to escape.

Straight from the Source!

Tandem enim totis imperii viribus contra myrmillonem consurgitur pudoremque Romanum Licinius Crassus adseruit; a quo pulsi fugatique—pudet dicere—hostes in extrema Italiae refugerunt. (Florus, Epitome Rerum Romanorum, 2.8.20.12-13)

Finally, even there was a surge against the gladiator with the entire strength of the empire and Licinius Crassus defended the Roman sense of honor; by which having been pushed out and put to flight—it is shameful to say—the enemy took refuge into the extremities of Italy. (Florus, Epitome Rerum Romanorum, 2.8.20.12-13)

  • Yet, Crassus eventually caught up with Spartacus and captured and crucified over 6000 men from Spartacus’s army.

Spartacus ipse in primo agmine fortissime dimicans quasi imperator occisus est. (Florus, Epitome Rerum Romanorum, 2.8.20.13)

Spartacus himself, fighting most bravely in the first battleline just like a general, was killed. (Florus, Epitome Rerum Romanorum, 2.8.20.13)
